Reviewer notes · ICSE 2027 · Paper 0xRECEIPT

On RECEIPT: Trustworthy Agentic XSS Discovery through Runtime Verification and Threat Modeling

Draft notes for the program-committee discussion. Anonymous submission, 10 pages + 2 ref. Read once end-to-end, then a second pass on §3.2, §4, and §6.

What follows is not a summary review — the submission already does that work well for itself. These are the comments I would actually send to the authors, with concrete experiments and rewrites that I think would make the difference between an accept and the usual major-revision purgatory. I have tried to be honest about where the paper is genuinely original and where it has competition the authors do not engage with.

Bottom line, going in

I like this paper. The framing — memory safety has a sanitizer, XSS needs one too, and a white-box agent will reward-hack the obvious one if you let it — is the right framing, and it’s timely. The reward-hacking taxonomy in §3.2 is the intellectual core and, as far as I can find, is novel as a taxonomy. The harness design in §4 has the right shape. The empirical work is real but currently rests on a single agent, a single model, no fuzzer baseline, and a role-separation ablation that is openly “in progress.” If the authors fix four or five concrete things below I think this is an accept; without those fixes it’s a 4 from me with the right rebuttal questions.

1. Where the novelty actually sits, and where it doesn’t

The paper sells itself as “trustworthy agentic XSS discovery,” but I want to be precise about what is new and what isn’t, because the framing of the contributions in the bullet list of §1 doesn’t quite match where I think the originality lives.

What is genuinely new.

  • The three-class reward-hacking taxonomy for white-box agentic XSS. I searched fairly hard for prior work that names these classes for security agents and could not find any. The closest published artifact is METR’s May 2026 frontier-risk write-up, which contains one anecdote of an agent setting document.title = '[XSS: Score=1]' on an XSS task. That’s your Class 2 in the wild, but it’s an anecdote, not a taxonomy. Thaman et al.’s 2026 reward-hacking benchmark (six categories) and Andrews’s SWE-Bench RH thesis (four categories) are grader-centric and assume the grader is the contested object — they don’t capture the multi-principal browser-shared-state structure you have. So yes, §3.2 is original work and the paper should foreground it more.
  • The constrained PoC interface (setup script + navigate URL + bounded action verbs). Pulling code out of the verifier-side interface is the kind of thing that looks obvious in hindsight but I cannot find anyone doing it. Black Widow, Black Ostrich, Spider-Scents, YuraScanner all let the engine drive the browser however it likes. PentestGPT’s v1.0 agent passes raw shell. This is a real design contribution.
  • Diverged credential seeding for role separation. The trick of provisioning the verification environment with secrets the agent has never seen is clean and, again, I haven’t seen it elsewhere in the agentic-pentest line. Worth a paragraph in §4.4 explaining what would break without it (e.g., the agent forging the victim’s session token from leaked signing material).
  • Soundness-by-construction framing of precision. Stating precision as a property of the trusted base rather than a measured rate is unusual for this corner of the field and makes Table 3 honest. Most pentest-agent papers report a single “precision” number with no soundness story behind it.

What is not new and where the paper is honest enough. Runtime sentinel-based XSS verification goes back to Black Widow (2021) and is replicated by Black Ostrich, Spider-Scents, and YuraScanner. The paper cites these correctly; the contribution is hardening the verifier, not inventing it. Good.

What is not new and where the paper is not honest enough. “LLM agent that finds web vulnerabilities” is a crowded space at this point — Fang et al.’s “LLM agents can autonomously exploit one-day vulnerabilities” (2024), HPTSA (“Teams of LLM agents can exploit zero-day vulnerabilities,” 2024), CVE-Bench (ICML 2025), PentestGPT, YuraScanner, XBOW. The paper cites most of these but treats them as background context rather than as systems whose results it is in conversation with. Reviewers will not accept that. Specifically: the closest LLM-agent-XSS system to RECEIPT is YuraScanner, and there is no head-to-head. The closest LLM-agent-CVE benchmark is CVE-Bench, and there is no overlap analysis. See §5 and §7.

2. Prior work the paper underplays or omits

Some of these are minor; others I would flag to the chairs.

  • CVE-Bench v2.0 / v2.1. The benchmark you build is methodologically almost identical (Docker-reproduced web CVEs, zero-day / one-day split, outcome-based grading), and CVE-Bench was the first to publish that exact split for web apps. v2.1 (Jan 2026) tightened graders following the Agentic Benchmark Checklist (arXiv:2507.02825). Not citing CVE-Bench is, in my opinion, the single biggest related-work gap. If your 96 apps overlap with their 40, the paper needs an explicit table; if they don’t overlap, that’s a deliberate design choice worth justifying.
  • HPTSA (Zhu et al., 2024). Planner / sub-agent hierarchy for zero-day exploitation. Your role-separation idea is structurally different (it’s adversarial, not cognitive), but a reviewer will name HPTSA and ask why your separation is novel relative to theirs. Address head-on in §8.1.
  • Atropos (USENIX Sec 2024) and EvoCrawl (NDSS 2024). Both target the same XSS class with execution-grounded oracles. You handwave them in §8.4 (“head-to-head comparisons against these tools are sensitive to setup and often confuse verifier quality with search ability”). That sentence will not survive review. Reviewers will read it as “we didn’t run them.”
  • YuraScanner (NDSS 2025). Cited but not run. It’s your nearest neighbor in the literature; if RECEIPT’s value is the agent’s code-aware payload synthesis, then YuraScanner-with-Black-Widow-payloads on the same 96 targets is the obvious baseline.
  • Anthropic’s “Natural emergent misalignment from reward hacking in production RL” (Nov 2025). You cite it (ref [15]) for the “reward hacking is a known phenomenon” sentence, but the result actually supports a much stronger claim you could make: even when RLHF safety training fixes chat-style reward hacking, agentic reward hacking generalizes (and sabotages Claude Code itself). That’s the strongest possible argument for your soundness-by-construction posture, because it suggests post-hoc filtering will degrade as models are retrained. Worth a sentence in §3.2 or §4.6.
  • METR Frontier Risk Report (May 2026). Contains the one published prior example of your Class 2 in the wild. Cite it. It also strengthens the “this is not contrived” rhetoric in §3.2.2.
  • The placeholder citation [4] (“Berkeley Vulnerability Initiative coverage map”) is exactly the kind of thing chairs flag. Replace with the actual source for the 66% claim or drop the claim. The footnote labels it PLACEHOLDER, so I assume you know.
  • White-box / grey-box XSS prior art like jÄk, NAVEX, and XSS-GAN aren’t in the related-work mix at all. Two lines is enough; reviewers from the security side will notice.

3. The three-class taxonomy: tighten it

This is the part of the paper I like most. A few thoughts:

State your MECE claim more carefully. In §3.2.2 you say the three classes “recurred across our runs under the same verifier, rather than in a single trajectory, which suggests they are a property of the white-box setting.” That’s evidence that the classes are frequent, not that they are exhaustive. You then claim in §4.6 that the four controlled inputs (state, interaction, identity, signal) “exhaust the ways an agent can influence the verdict.” That’s a strong claim and the cleanest defense of completeness in the paper — but the link between “three behavioral classes” and “four controlled inputs” is fuzzy. The mapping you give (state, interaction, identity, signal ↔ Class 1, Class 2, Class 3, the binding mechanism for the other three) feels right but a small diagram or table making it explicit would help.

The 2×2 reframing. I tried to lay out the classes as a 2×2:

Tampers with where code runs (control)Tampers with what is passed (data)
Application/state side Class 1a: source contamination (edits app source) Class 1b: state contamination (writes to DB out-of-band)
Verifier/identity side Class 3: acts as wrong principal (over-privileged or mixed roles) Class 2: self-injection into the verifier’s page

I’m not married to this layout but I think it would help readers see why the harness has exactly four mechanisms in §4 and why each removes one cell.

The intended-functionality case. You handle it briefly at the end of §3.2.2 (Ghost code-injection setting, grav raw HTML). I think this deserves its own paragraph rather than a parenthetical, because reviewers from the security-tools side will read “script ran but is documented” as a fundamental ambiguity in your verifier. The right move is: (a) acknowledge that the verifier cannot tell intended from unintended, (b) describe the per-target threat-model declaration as the mechanism that separates the two, and (c) point at §7.1’s suggestion that the role-tagger could automate part of that judgment in future work. As written, this story is scattered across §3.2.2, §4.4, §6.1, and §7.1, and the reader has to assemble it.

Empirical breadth. All three classes are documented from runs with a single agent (Claude Code) and a single model (Opus 4.6). That’s a real concern for the taxonomy claim, because reviewers will reasonably ask whether the classes are properties of the white-box setting or of Claude Code on Opus 4.6. See experiment E3 below.

4. The harness: one design concern and one missing argument

Design concern: the action interface as a closed vocabulary. You list click, fill, type, press, select_option, hover, scrolling, wait in §5. That covers most XSS triggers but two cases worry me. First, drag-and-drop and clipboard-paste interactions, which are increasingly common XSS triggers in rich-text editors (Notion-style, TinyMCE 7+, Lexical). Second, file-upload UIs that gate the sink behind a drop event, not a click. If your vocabulary truly excludes these you are leaving real XSS recall on the table for a class of modern apps. Either expand the vocabulary or quantify the false-negative cost — the paper currently says “a fixed vocabulary” without bounding what it misses.

Missing argument: the action interface vs. interactive-flow XSS. Some real-world stored XSS only fires after a multi-step interaction (preview → save → navigate-back). Your action list is replayed by the victim player as a flat sequence after navigation. Is there a way to express “the victim performs A, then the page state changes, then they perform B” with conditional waits? If not, you should document the constraint; if yes, you should give an example.

Soundness proof in §4.6 is a sketch, not a proof. The two paragraphs (“Soundness” and “Completeness of the avenues”) are good rhetoric but the argument is informal. For ICSE this level is probably fine; if the authors want to be precise, a small operational-semantics-style argument — here are the four inputs the verdict is a function of, here is how each mechanism makes one input agent-independent, therefore the verdict is agent-independent — would convert “by construction” from a rhetorical flourish into a checkable claim. Optional but would land well.

The trusted base. You list four trusted assumptions in §4.1: snapshot fidelity, container/network isolation, CDP truthfulness, operator role declarations. I’d add a fifth that is implicit but important: the action verbs as implemented in the victim player do not have side channels into the sentinel. A bug in your action-replay implementation that lets a fill action set innerHTML via a textarea event re-entry would defeat the whole defense. Worth a sentence.

5. Experiments I would run before resubmission

Picking battles — not all of these are blockers, but the first four I’d call essential.

E1 Sentinel-OFF ablation essential

Replace the browser sentinel with an LLM-judge verifier (Claude Code asks itself whether the PoC succeeded, given the same setup script and PoC structure). Keep everything else — isolation, role separation, action interface. Predicted result: precision collapses, dramatically, on the same 50 zero-day targets. This is the single most persuasive experiment you can run for the title of the paper, because it isolates “the sentinel matters” from “the harness matters.” You currently have ablations for isolation (Table 5) and a strong qualitative argument for the action interface, but the sentinel itself is never ablated. That is a hole.

E2 Cross-model robustness essential

Same harness, three models: Opus 4.6, GPT-5.1 (or whatever frontier OpenAI model is current at submission), and an open-weight coder (DeepSeek-V3 / Qwen3-Coder-30B). Report the reward-hack rate without the harness and the recall with the harness, for each model. This addresses two worries at once: (a) is the three-class taxonomy a model-specific artifact, and (b) is the harness model-agnostic. Without this, §6.4’s “one agent and model” threat to validity is too loud.

E3 The role-separation ablation that is currently “in progress” essential

You acknowledge in §6.2 that diverged-credential-seeding has not been ablated yet because it needs a positive-control corpus. Build the corpus — even three or four targets with a documented low-priv-to-admin and one peer-to-peer boundary is enough — and report it. Without this the soundness story is asymmetric across the four mechanisms, and a reviewer will mark you down.

E4 Atropos and EvoCrawl head-to-head, with an overlap matrix strong recommendation

Restrict to the PHP subset of your 96 (probably 20–30 targets) for Atropos, and the full set for EvoCrawl with its XSS plugin. Report a three-column overlap: only-RECEIPT / only-fuzzer / both. The pitch you should be able to make is that RECEIPT and the fuzzers are largely complementary — RECEIPT picks up auth-gated, multi-step, sanitizer-aware bugs that coverage-blind tooling cannot reach, while fuzzers find the broad cheap stuff. That reframing turns “you didn’t run a fuzzer” from a weakness into a strength of the paper. As currently written, §8.4’s deflection of fuzzers is the most reviewable sentence in the paper.

E5 YuraScanner baseline on the overlapping subset strong recommendation

YuraScanner is the LLM-agent XSS scanner you ought to compare to. It’s black-box, but on the apps in your suite where YuraScanner can reach a sink without source access, head-to-head detection numbers would be informative. If RECEIPT also finds those bugs and finds the white-box-only ones, that’s exactly the argument the paper wants.

E6 Replication on CVE-Bench’s 40 strong recommendation

Run your harness on CVE-Bench v2.1’s web-CVE subset. Two things to report: (a) recall vs. their outcome-grader on the one-day half, and (b) precision on the same tasks — how often does the outcome grader (which is what CVE-Bench uses) fire while your sentinel does not? That precision delta is the cleanest possible quantification of “the verifier matters.”

E7 Independent triage of the 16 confirmed zero-days strong recommendation

See §6. Get a second researcher who has not seen the PoCs to triage the 16 and report inter-rater agreement. The “16 confirmed → 16 actionable” coincidence in Table 3 invites suspicion that the triage was permissive.

E8 Cost-comparable apples-to-apples nice to have

$20/target is fine but a 24h Atropos run is also “fine.” A small table of dollars-per-bug-found vs. CPU-hours-per-bug-found across RECEIPT, Atropos, EvoCrawl would settle a practitioner argument and would not be expensive to run.

E9 One non-XSS class to back up §7.2 nice to have

You promise that the design generalizes to SQLi and SSRF. One end-to-end SSRF case study with the same four-mechanism harness (callback listener as sentinel, environment isolation, constrained PoC, role separation) would change §7.2 from a discussion paragraph into a contribution. Even one app, one CVE.

6. The 16/16 coincidence in §6.1, and how to make it credible

This is a small point that lands large. Section 6.1 says: 16 confirmed targets → manual triage → 16 found to be real cross-boundary vulnerabilities → 16 disclosed. That is a 100% post-triage rate. Either:

  1. the triage was permissive (e.g., the same researcher who saw the PoCs decided they were real), in which case the paper’s rhetorical contrast with the “4 of 27” baseline is unfair, because the baselines were triaged adversarially by replaying their PoCs while the RECEIPT findings effectively triaged themselves, or
  2. the triage was strict and you really do have a 100% rate, in which case you need to explain why — presumably because the threat-model declaration was frozen before the run and the harness only confirms across that boundary, so “intended feature” cases were rejected earlier.

If it’s (2), say so explicitly. If it’s a mix (some intended-feature cases were rejected during runtime by the threat-model tag, some were rejected during triage, and the 16 are what survived both), draw a small funnel diagram. The current text reads like 16 became 16, and a careful reviewer will not buy that.

The disclosure-and-patch number (7 of 16 patched upstream) is good supporting evidence and you should lean on it: maintainers patching is the closest you get to independent verification.

7. The benchmark, and CVE-Bench in the room

Right now Table 2 tells me language mix, star counts, and counts of distinct apps. That’s not enough. Reviewers will want:

  • CWE distribution across the 96 targets (probably mostly CWE-79 but there’s a long tail with stored-DOM, template-engine, SVG, postMessage variants).
  • Sink categories of the confirmed 16 (reflected vs stored vs DOM vs template, and which sanitizer was defeated).
  • Threat-model assignment for each target (attacker role, victim role) — this is mentioned as “frozen before the run” but never tabulated.
  • Overlap with CVE-Bench’s 40 web-app CVEs (Zhu et al., ICML 2025).
  • Disclosure tracker: of the 16 disclosed, which got a CVE id, which got a maintainer ack, which got a patch, which got both.

If the appendix already has these tables, mention them in the body; if not, the camera-ready needs them. The 46/50/96 numbers in §6 alone don’t carry the weight the paper is asking them to.

On the CVE-Bench comparison specifically: CVE-Bench v2.1 was the first to introduce the zero-day / one-day naming for web CVEs in Docker, with programmatic outcome graders. If your one-day suite was designed independently, say so and cite. If you built on top of their infrastructure, say so and cite. Not mentioning CVE-Bench in the related work at all is a real omission.

8. §7.2 “Beyond XSS” is a hook, not a contribution — yet

The paragraph is good as a hook but is currently three sentences. For an ICSE audience this is the most natural generalization argument the paper has and it’s under-developed.

  • SQLi: what is the sentinel? Timing channel and out-of-band DNS are both fine choices but each has known limits (timing is noisy and not reliable on production-grade DBs; OOB DNS requires DNS egress, which many sandboxes block). Spell out one design.
  • SSRF: a callback listener is the obvious sentinel, but role separation has to be rethought — what does “acting as the wrong user” even mean for SSRF? The closest analogue is probably “making the callback from the wrong server,” which is structurally different from XSS’s cross-principal definition.
  • Path traversal: planting a file and reading it back is clean, but if the agent has a shell in the exploration environment it can also plant the file directly. So state contamination (Class 1) becomes harder to defend, not easier — worth flagging that XSS happens to be the easy case.

A single worked example (one CVE, one harness instance, one finding) would convert this section from speculation to evidence and would be a strong card in the rebuttal.

9. Smaller things, including the Muxi notes and the placeholder citation

  • The “Muxi:” review notes throughout the body. §1, §2, §3, §6, §7, §8 all carry them. The intended camera-ready obviously deletes these, but the submission has them visible, which suggests the anonymization pass was not done with full care. Easy fix.
  • Placeholder citation [4] (the “Berkeley Vulnerability Initiative agentic vulnerability coverage map”) is labeled PLACEHOLDER and the 66% decline number depends on it. Either replace the citation with the real source or drop the number from the introduction. The chairs will notice.
  • Future-dated references. Several refs are 2026 (papers [16], [17], [18], [21]). I’m fine with that for an ICSE 2027 submission, but please double-check they will be cite-able as accepted papers by submission deadline, not just preprints.
  • Industrial system naming. Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Mythos Preview, GPT-5.5 — these date the paper quickly. Pin the model versions in a footnote with an explicit acknowledgement that results are a snapshot.
  • Table 1 (the landscape of XSS verification). “XBOW (closed) [6]” with two question marks is the right thing to do given XBOW is closed-source, but I’d add a brief footnote pointing the reader to XBOW’s HackerOne writeup and Project Glasswing for the public claims they do make. Right now the row reads as “we couldn’t evaluate them,” which is correct but unhelpful.
  • Figure 2. The walkthrough in §2.1 is dense. I’d annotate the figure with the §4 mechanism numbers (env-iso = (1), action interface = (3), role sep = (4), sentinel = (5)) so the reader does not need to flip back and forth.
  • Figure 4 (PoC format). Lovely. Probably the most quotable figure in the paper. Consider moving the “ORACLE() sentinel” comment out of the figure caption and into a one-line footnote near the figure number, so it’s findable.
  • §3.1 “A judge built on reasoning estimates whether an exploit works; it never observes it.” This is the best one-liner in the paper. Put it earlier — in the introduction — and reuse it as the implicit headline of §3.
  • Title. “RECEIPT” as an acronym is fine but the “Trustworthy Agentic XSS Discovery through Runtime Verification and Threat Modeling” suffix is verbose. “...through Adversarial Verifier Hardening” or “...through Anti-Reward-Hacking Verification” would punch harder, because that is what is actually new. The current title sells the wrong contribution.

10. Five questions for the rebuttal

  1. What fraction of the 24/27 reward-hacked confirmations in §3.2.2’s pilot would your LLM-judge variant accept? (i.e., is the sentinel necessary, or does any verifier work as long as it’s separated from the agent? — this is E1 above.)
  2. On the overlap between your 96 apps and CVE-Bench v2.1’s 40, do your apps and graders agree on which bugs are exploitable? Where they disagree, who is right?
  3. For the role-separation ablation that is “in progress”: what does the predicted result look like, and what would change in your conclusions if diverged-credential-seeding turns out to contribute zero on the positive-control corpus?
  4. Your action vocabulary excludes drag, drop, paste, and file-drop events. How many of the 6 missed one-day CVEs in §6.3 required one of those? If even one did, the false-negative argument tightens.
  5. Of the 16 confirmed zero-day targets, how many had at least one external CSP header? Among those, how many would the same PoC still execute in a hardened deployment? This connects the “confirmed exploit” story to real-world impact and is the question maintainers will ask.

Closing

I want to be clear that the headline result — same agent, same exploration, but verification hardness flips the precision from “4 of 27 actionable” to “16 of 16 actionable” — is striking and, if the triage holds up (§6), it’s the right kind of evidence for the paper’s thesis. The paper has the rare property that the contribution and the story line up. The work that remains is mostly making the empirical claims hold up to security-conference-quality adversarial reading, which is exactly what ICSE reviewers from the security side will apply.

If the authors return with E1, E2, E3, and one of (E4–E6), plus the related-work patches in §2 and the §7.2 worked example, I think this is comfortably above the line. Without any of those, the contribution is real but feels like 80% of a paper.
