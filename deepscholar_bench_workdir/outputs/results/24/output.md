# Related Works

## Diffusion Models for Image and Video Generation

Diffusion models have become a dominant paradigm for high-fidelity generative modeling, starting with denoising diffusion probabilistic models [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.11239) and their latent-space extension for scalable image synthesis [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.10752). Peebles and Xie replaced U-Net backbones with Diffusion Transformers (DiT), demonstrating that transformer architectures scale gracefully with compute and data [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.09748). Building on this line of research, video diffusion models extend the denoising formulation from images to spatiotemporal signals [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.03458), enabling text-conditioned systems such as Make-A-Video [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.14792) and Imagen Video [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.02303), as well as large open-source models like Stable Video Diffusion [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.15127). More recently, transformer-based video generators such as Latte [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03048), Open-Sora [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.20404), and CogVideoX [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.06072) have set new quality benchmarks, but their billions of parameters and multi-step sampling procedures make deployment on edge devices prohibitive. Benchmarks such as VBench [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.17982) further highlight the need to preserve spatiotemporal consistency in compressed models, a criterion that motivates our Temporal Maintenance Distillation objective.

## Post-Training Quantization for Neural Networks

Post-training quantization (PTQ) compresses pretrained networks without full retraining and is the standard route to efficient deployment. AdaRound formulated weight rounding as a per-layer optimization problem [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.10568), while BRECQ generalized this to block-wise reconstruction with second-order guidance [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.05426). Learned Step Size Quantization introduced differentiable step sizes for low-bit inference [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1902.08153). For large language models, GPTQ [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.17323), AWQ [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.00978), and SmoothQuant [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.10438) address activation outliers and weight-only compression, providing algorithmic templates that later PTQ methods for diffusion models have adapted. Our Token-aware Quantization Estimator draws inspiration from these outlier-aware and reconstruction-based formulations, but reformulates the error compensation along the token and feature axes that are characteristic of transformer video generators.

## Quantization of Diffusion Models

A growing body of work targets diffusion-specific quantization. PTQ4DM introduced calibration strategies that account for the multi-step denoising process [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.15736), and Q-Diffusion further tackled activation distribution shifts across timesteps [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.04304). PTQD analyzes and corrects the biased noise introduced by quantization to preserve sample quality [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.10657), while EfficientDM proposes low-rank quantization-aware fine-tuning that avoids expensive full retraining [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03270). TFMQ-DM identifies that temporal features across denoising steps carry critical information and maintains them explicitly during quantization [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.16503); this observation motivates our distillation objective, which extends the notion of temporal consistency from denoising steps to video frames.

## Quantization of Diffusion Transformers and Video Models

The transition from convolutional diffusion U-Nets to DiT backbones introduces new challenges such as attention outliers, unique normalization schemes, and long token sequences. PTQ4DiT was one of the first works to tailor PTQ to diffusion transformers and reported large accuracy gains over generic PTQ baselines [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.16005). Q-DiT further improves calibration through fine-grained group quantization and adaptive search over rounding decisions [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.17343). HQ-DiT explores FP4 hybrid quantization for DiT under aggressive bit-widths [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.19751). ViDiT-Q [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.02540) is the closest prior effort to ours, extending PTQ to both image and video DiTs by handling token- and timestep-wise activation variance. However, these methods primarily inherit optimization objectives from image generation and do not explicitly model the spatiotemporal correlations between frames. In contrast, Q-VDiT combines a token-aware error estimator with a temporal maintenance distillation loss so that quantization errors are compensated at the granularity of individual tokens while every frame is optimized under the context of the full video, achieving state-of-the-art scene consistency at W3A6 precision.
