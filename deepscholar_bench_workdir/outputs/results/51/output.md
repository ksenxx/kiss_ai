# Related Work

## Federated Learning and Online Learning to Rank

Federated learning (FL) enables collaborative model training across decentralized clients without sharing raw data, and was introduced by the seminal FedAvg algorithm [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.05629). The paradigm has since expanded rapidly, with a comprehensive survey outlining open problems around privacy, robustness, personalization, and system heterogeneity [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.04977). Within information retrieval, Online Learning to Rank (OLTR) traditionally optimizes rankers directly from user interaction signals such as clicks, with counterfactual and unbiased LTR methods providing propensity-weighted objectives to combat position and selection bias [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.04468). Standard OLTR benchmarks such as LETOR 4.0 [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1306.2597) are commonly used to evaluate ranking effectiveness in this setting.

Federated Online Learning to Rank (FOLTR) marries these two lines of work: search rankers are trained collaboratively across users while implicit feedback and queries remain on-device. Recent work systematically studies FOLTR effectiveness and privacy, including a thesis-length treatment of secure and effective federated ranking with algorithms such as FPDGD and its variants [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19069), and dedicated analyses of how non-IID click behavior across clients affects ranking convergence and generalization [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.09272). Our study builds directly on these FOLTR foundations by evaluating what happens when clients want to *leave* the federation and have their contributions erased.

## Machine Unlearning

The right to be forgotten, codified by regulations such as GDPR, has motivated a rich literature on *machine unlearning*: the removal of the influence of specific training data from a trained model. Early work formalized data deletion for simple models and clustering [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.05012) and provided certified data removal guarantees for convex learners via influence-function corrections [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.03030). The SISA framework achieves exact unlearning by sharding and slicing training data so that only affected shards need retraining [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.03817). Broader surveys categorize these approaches, contrasting exact vs. approximate unlearning, and highlight the difficulty of both defining and verifying successful forgetting [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.02299) [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.03558).

## Federated Unlearning

Federated unlearning inherits the challenges of centralized unlearning while adding constraints specific to FL: the server has no direct access to raw data, historical updates may be stale, and coordination across clients is expensive. FedEraser reconstructs an unlearned global model by calibrating cached historical client updates, avoiding full retraining [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.13891). Halimi et al. propose an efficient client-level erasure procedure based on projected gradient ascent within a bounded reference ball, followed by a short federated fine-tuning phase [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.05521). Liu et al. cast the right to be forgotten as a rapid-retraining problem using diagonal empirical Fisher information for accelerated recovery [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.07320), and knowledge-distillation-based federated unlearning erases class- or client-specific knowledge while preserving overall model utility [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.09441). Two recent surveys consolidate this fast-moving landscape, examining unlearning granularities, design guidelines, and evaluation metrics [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.05146) [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.20448), and comparative reviews further contrast method families along efficiency, effectiveness, and threat-model dimensions [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.19218). To our knowledge, none of these methods have been rigorously benchmarked in the specifically online, ranking-oriented FOLTR setting that we investigate.

## Verification and Evaluation of Unlearning

A central challenge in the unlearning literature is *verifying* that data has actually been forgotten. Membership inference attacks (MIAs), pioneered by Shokri et al., train shadow models to distinguish training members from non-members and are a natural probe for residual data influence [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.05820). Modern likelihood-ratio attacks such as LiRA sharpen these tests and expose the fragility of privacy claims [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.03570). Chen et al. show that unlearning can paradoxically *increase* leakage by exposing differences between the original and unlearned models [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.02205). SCRUB and related work introduce approximate unlearners that explicitly balance forgetting and retention, and evaluate them jointly with MIAs [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.09880), while Hayes et al. warn that inexact unlearning procedures can create a false sense of privacy when only single, weak metrics are reported [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.01218). Backdoor-based verification embeds triggers into a subset of a client's data so that the disappearance of the trigger-induced behavior serves as evidence of erasure [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.04247); VeriFi extends this idea to the federated setting by giving departing clients cryptographic and behavioral tools to audit the server's compliance [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.12709). Nonetheless, recent analysis demonstrates that both reproducing-based and backdoor-based verification pipelines can be circumvented by adversarial unlearners, arguing for multi-metric evaluation regimes [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.00929). Our paper responds to this call by systematically combining backdoor-verification, MIA-based tests, and ranking-utility metrics to jointly diagnose under-unlearning and over-unlearning in FOLTR.
