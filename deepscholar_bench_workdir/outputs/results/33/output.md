# Related Work

**Catastrophic forgetting and continual learning.** The tendency of neural networks to abruptly lose previously acquired knowledge when trained on new tasks — catastrophic forgetting — has been a central obstacle to continual learning (CL) since it was systematically documented in modern deep networks by [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1312.6211). Subsequent surveys have organized the resulting literature into three broad families — regularization-based, architecture-based, and rehearsal-based approaches — and have benchmarked their stability–plasticity trade-offs [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07569), [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.08383). Our work fits within this taxonomy but focuses specifically on when and how past data should be revisited.

**Regularization-based methods.** A first line of defense against forgetting adds penalties that discourage large changes to parameters deemed important for prior tasks. Elastic Weight Consolidation uses the Fisher information matrix to slow learning on task-critical weights [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.00796), while Synaptic Intelligence accumulates an online path-integral estimate of per-synapse importance during training [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.04200). These methods avoid storing data but are known to degrade when tasks are numerous or dissimilar, which motivates the memory-based approaches we build on.

**Rehearsal-based (replay) methods.** Rehearsal methods store a small subset of past examples and interleave them with current-task data during training. iCaRL couples exemplar herding with a distillation loss to learn class-incremental representations [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.07725). Gradient Episodic Memory (GEM) projects the current gradient so it never increases loss on stored past samples, and introduces the standard average-accuracy, backward-transfer and forward-transfer metrics used throughout CL [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.08840); Averaged-GEM (A-GEM) makes this constraint dramatically cheaper by using only the average memory gradient [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.00420). CLEAR shows that in reinforcement learning a simple replay buffer combined with behavioral cloning already eliminates most forgetting [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.11682). Dark Experience Replay (DER/DER++) additionally stores historical logits and matches them via distillation, providing one of the strongest general-CL baselines [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.07211). Meta-Experience Replay (MER) reframes replay as a transfer-versus-interference trade-off, using Reptile-style meta-updates to explicitly align gradients between new and rehearsed examples [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.11910). All of these methods share a *concurrent* rehearsal schedule: past and new samples are optimized together in the same batches. Our work questions whether this joint schedule is always optimal and studies *sequential* revisiting as an alternative.

**Theoretical analyses of continual and rehearsal-based learning.** A recent line of work analyzes CL in tractable models to isolate the roles of overparameterization, task similarity, and memory. In the Neural Tangent Kernel regime, closed-form expressions have been derived for orthogonal-gradient-descent variants, yielding the first generalization guarantees against catastrophic forgetting [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.11942), and analogous NTK-based analyses have been used to guide parameter-efficient fine-tuning for CL [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.17120). Overparameterized linear regression has proved especially fruitful: [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.10442) shows that overparameterization alone can already mitigate forgetting under vanilla gradient descent, while [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.12617) derives an exact closed-form for expected forgetting jointly as a function of task similarity and model width, uncovering non-monotone regimes where intermediate similarity is worst. Closest in spirit to our study, [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.16939) analyzes multi-task and replay-based CL in overparameterized linear models, quantifying how buffer size, model capacity, and task similarity govern generalization and forgetting. Our analysis complements these works by being, to our knowledge, the first to theoretically compare *concurrent* and *sequential* rehearsal schedules in overparameterized linear models, to characterize the task-similarity regime in which each is preferred, and to derive from this comparison a hybrid rehearsal algorithm that we validate on deep networks.
