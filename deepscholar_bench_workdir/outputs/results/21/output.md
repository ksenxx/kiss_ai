# Related Work

**Neural Rendering for Novel View Synthesis.** Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.08934) sparked a wave of coordinate-based scene representations that recover volumetric geometry and appearance from posed multi-view images. Follow-up work has focused on quality and efficiency: Mip-NeRF [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.13415) introduces cone-tracing to combat aliasing, Mip-NeRF 360 [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.12077) extends the formulation to unbounded scenes, and Instant-NGP [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.05989) accelerates training via multi-resolution hash encodings. More recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.04079) has emerged as an explicit, real-time alternative that retains high visual fidelity. Despite these advances, all such methods fundamentally rely on multi-view photometric supervision, and their quality degrades sharply when the input views are sparse or cover only a limited range of viewpoints—the setting our work targets.

**Sparse-View and Few-Shot Neural Rendering.** A large body of work attempts to compensate for missing views through learned priors or regularization. pixelNeRF [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.02190) and MVSNeRF [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.15595) condition NeRF on image features to enable feed-forward reconstruction from one or a few images. DietNeRF [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.00677) enforces semantic consistency across unseen viewpoints using CLIP embeddings, while RegNeRF [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.00724) regularizes geometry and appearance at unobserved views via a patch-based normalizing flow. FreeNeRF [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07418) demonstrates that carefully scheduling frequency bands alone can substantially improve few-shot rendering. Our approach is complementary: rather than constraining the optimization, we synthesize additional pixel-accurate supervision that can be dropped into any of these pipelines.

**Dynamic Scene Reconstruction.** Extending neural rendering to time-varying content further exacerbates the sparse-supervision problem, since each moment is observed by only a handful of cameras (or a single moving camera). Deformation-based models such as D-NeRF [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.13961), Nerfies [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.12948), and HyperNeRF [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.13228) warp a canonical field, while Neural Scene Flow Fields [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.13084) predict per-point 3D motion. In the Gaussian-splatting family, Dynamic 3D Gaussians [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.09713), Deformable 3D Gaussians [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.13101), and 4D Gaussian Splatting [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.08528) track motion through explicit primitives. These methods still assume dense temporal sampling; frame interpolation provides a natural, geometry-aware way to densify their temporal supervision.

**Data Augmentation and Generative Priors for 3D.** Several works enrich neural rendering with additional supervisory signal. Aug-NeRF [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.01164) injects physically grounded perturbations during training, and GANeRF [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.06044) leverages adversarial discriminators to sharpen renderings. GeCoNeRF [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.10941) uses geometric consistency between rendered patches as pseudo-labels, whereas DiffusioNeRF [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.12231), NeRDi [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.03267), and ReconFusion [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02981) distill 2D diffusion priors to hallucinate plausible unseen views. Diffusion-based augmentation, however, is computationally heavy and can introduce content inconsistent with the observed scene. In contrast, our video-interpolation prior is lightweight and stays anchored to the captured pixels, functioning as an implicit world model that respects the true camera trajectory.

**Video Frame Interpolation.** Video frame interpolation (VFI) has evolved from kernel-based synthesis with SepConv [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/1708.01692) and multi-frame flow estimation in Super SloMo [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.00080), to depth-aware flow warping in DAIN [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.00830), and to real-time flow-based synthesis with RIFE [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.06294). FILM [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.04901) tackles large-motion "near-duplicate" interpolation, while VFIformer [29](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.07230), IFRNet [30](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.14620), EMA-VFI [31](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.00440), AMT [32](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.09790), and BiFormer [33](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.02225) push accuracy and efficiency further with transformer and all-pairs formulations. Although VFI is typically evaluated for slow-motion generation, these models implicitly learn strong priors over camera motion and scene geometry—priors that we repurpose in PS4PRO to densify multi-view captures for neural rendering.
