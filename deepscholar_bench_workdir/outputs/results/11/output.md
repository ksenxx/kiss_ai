# Related Works

## 2D-to-3D Lifting for Monocular Human Pose Estimation

A dominant paradigm for monocular 3D human pose estimation decouples the task into 2D keypoint detection followed by a 2D-to-3D lifting stage. Martinez et al. [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.03098) showed that a surprisingly simple fully-connected network can lift ground-truth 2D joints to 3D locations with strong accuracy, establishing the "lifting" baseline that most subsequent work builds on. Because per-frame lifting is ill-posed due to depth ambiguity and self-occlusion, later methods enrich the input with temporal context. Pavllo et al. [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.11742) proposed temporal dilated convolutions over long 2D sequences and introduced a semi-supervised back-projection loss. Cheng et al. [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.11822) explicitly modelled occlusions in a spatio-temporal network, while Wang et al. [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.13985) exploited motion cues from optical-flow-like trajectories to guide the lifting. Trajectory Space Factorization [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.08289) further decomposes joint trajectories into learnable bases. Chen et al. [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.10322) reformulated the task as bone-based anatomical decomposition, injecting structural priors about the human skeleton — a motivation shared with our pyramid grouping.

## Graph Convolutions on Human Skeletons

Because the human skeleton is naturally a graph, graph convolutional networks (GCNs) have become a standard tool for pose regression. Zhao et al.'s SemGCN [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.03345) endows GCNs with learnable, semantically weighted edges so that joints can influence one another beyond the physical bone connections. Yan et al.'s ST-GCN [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.07455) extends this idea to space-time skeleton graphs for action-related tasks, providing the temporal-spatial graph formulation many later 3D pose networks reuse. Velickovic et al.'s Graph Attention Networks [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1710.10903) replace fixed adjacency weights with attention coefficients, enabling data-dependent message passing between joints and inspiring the graph-attention branch of our Pyramid Graph Transformer. A key limitation of these vanilla graph designs is that only physically linked joints exchange information at each layer; capturing dependencies between non-linked parts (e.g., left hand and right foot during walking) traditionally requires stacking many layers, which introduces noise and inflates model size — precisely the pathology our pyramid mechanism is designed to avoid.

## Transformer-based 3D Pose Estimation

Following the transformer's success in language and vision [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762), attention has been adapted to human pose. PoseFormer [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.10455) is a purely transformer-based spatio-temporal architecture that jointly models per-frame joint relations and cross-frame temporal correlations. MHFormer [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.12707) explicitly generates and refines multiple plausible pose hypotheses to cope with depth ambiguity. Li et al.'s Strided Transformer [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.14304) introduces temporal down-sampling to model longer contexts at reduced cost. MixSTE [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.00859) alternates spatial and temporal encoders so that each joint carries its own temporal trajectory, and P-STMO [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.07628) pre-trains a many-to-one spatio-temporal encoder via masked joint modelling. Although these transformers can, in principle, capture long-range dependencies, they treat joints as independent tokens and therefore do not natively encode the hierarchical, part-based organisation of the human body that PGFormer aims to exploit.

## Multi-scale and Pyramid Architectures

Pyramid and multi-scale designs have a long history in dense prediction. Feature Pyramid Networks [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.03144) combine features across resolutions for object detection, while the Pyramid Scene Parsing Network [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.01105) aggregates global context via pyramid pooling for segmentation. In 2D human pose, HRNet [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1902.09212) keeps high-resolution representations throughout the network and fuses multi-resolution streams, and HigherHRNet [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.10357) extends this idea with scale-aware deconvolution for bottom-up estimation. These works show that reasoning across scales is a powerful alternative to simply deepening the network. Our PGFormer transfers this intuition to skeleton graphs: by pooling joints into progressively coarser sub-structures and applying cross-scale graph attention, it captures dependencies between individual joints and body-part groups within a compact, lightweight architecture, in contrast to prior GCN- and transformer-based pose estimators [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.03345),[11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.10455),[12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.12707) that operate at a single joint-level scale.
