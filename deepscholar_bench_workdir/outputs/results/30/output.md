# Related Work

**Isolation levels and consistency specifications.**
The theoretical foundations of weak isolation trace back to axiomatic frameworks that formalize what transactions may observe. Bouajjani, Enea, Guerraoui, and Hamza gave the first declarative characterization of causal consistency for transactional systems and studied its verification [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.00580). Highly-available transactional models such as HAT further motivated the study of weak-but-scalable isolation guarantees by showing which levels remain achievable under network partitions [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1302.0309), and subsequent work explored fundamental latency/consistency trade-offs for causal protocols [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.04237). These specifications frame the isolation levels — Read Committed, Read Atomic, and Causal Consistency — that our tester targets.

**Complexity of transactional consistency checking.**
A line of work has mapped the complexity landscape of black-box isolation checking. Biswas and Enea established that checking serializability, snapshot isolation, and prefix consistency is NP-complete, while showing polynomial-time algorithms for the weaker Read Atomic and Causal Consistency levels [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.04509). Zennou et al. investigated efficient certificates and reductions for causal consistency checking of distributed databases [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.09753). Our work sharpens this landscape: we provide the first sub-quadratic algorithms for RC and RA in $O(n^{3/2})$ time, and a linear-in-$n$, $O(n\cdot k)$ algorithm for CC, matched by a conditional $n^{3/2}$ lower bound derived via reduction from Boolean Matrix Multiplication.

**Practical black-box isolation testers.**
Several tools have applied consistency checking to production databases. Elle infers isolation anomalies from experimental observations produced by Jepsen-style fault injection [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.10554). Cobra makes serializability checking practical using SMT with pruning heuristics and GPU-accelerated preprocessing [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.09018). Follow-up work has targeted specific levels — for example, efficient black-box snapshot-isolation checking that reduces the search space via graph-based encodings [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.07313) — while IsoPredict extends checking with dynamic predictive analysis to find serializability bugs in weakly isolated applications [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.04621). Concurrent to us, mini-transaction based encodings have been proposed to accelerate end-to-end isolation checking pipelines [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02344). While these tools broaden the applicability of isolation testing, most incur large polynomial (or worst-case exponential) overheads. AWDIT complements this line by providing provably optimal algorithms specifically for weak isolation, delivering multi-hundred-fold speedups on large histories.

**Consistency checking under weak memory models.**
Testing database isolation is closely related to verifying reads-from executions under weak memory models. Chakraborty, Krishna, Mathur, and Pavlogiannis established fine-grained complexity bounds for weak-memory testing under models such as SC, TSO, PSO, and RA [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.04302). Tunç et al. gave optimal reads-from checking for C11-style memory models [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03714), while Bui et al. studied the reads-from equivalence for TSO/PSO in the context of stateless model checking [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.11763). These works share the algorithmic core of reasoning about read-write dependencies subject to session/program order.

**Dynamic analysis and partial-order data structures.**
Our polynomial-time upper bounds build on techniques developed for dynamic concurrency analysis. Mathur, Pavlogiannis, and Viswanathan characterized the complexity of dynamic data race prediction [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.14931), Pavlogiannis presented sound and effectively complete race prediction with strong guarantees [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/1901.08857), and follow-up data structures — tree clocks [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.06325) and collective sparse segment trees (CSSTs) [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.17818) — enable near-linear maintenance of partial orders. Data-centric DPOR further exploits read/write structure to reduce the explored state space [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.01188). AWDIT adapts these ideas to obtain efficient, cache-friendly algorithms tailored to weak database isolation.
