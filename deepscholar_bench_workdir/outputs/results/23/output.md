# Related Works

## LiDAR–Camera Extrinsic Calibration
Learning-based extrinsic calibration was pioneered by RegNet [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.03167), which regresses the 6-DoF rigid transformation between a LiDAR and a camera directly from paired depth and RGB inputs using a deep convolutional network. CalibNet [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.08181) removed the need for explicit correspondence labels by introducing a geometrically supervised loss based on 3D spatial transformers, and CalibDNN [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.14793) further simplified the pipeline into a single-model, single-iteration target-less predictor. LCCNet [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.13901) demonstrated that constructing a *cost volume* between projected LiDAR features and image features is a strong inductive bias for extrinsic regression, and CFNet [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.11907) recast the problem as predicting a dense 2D "calibration flow" that is then resolved through EPnP+RANSAC. More recent works have moved toward stronger geometric or semantic supervision: MIAS-LCEC performs online, target-free LiDAR–camera calibration by cross-modal *mask matching* built on large vision models [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.18083); TLC-Calib jointly optimizes sensor poses with a neural Gaussian-splatting scene representation [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.04597); and a recent benchmark study analyzes which design choices actually matter for learning-based LiDAR–camera calibration [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16969). These methods provide the strongest baselines against which the proposed radar–camera approach is compared, but they all rely on dense, height-accurate depth measurements that radar does not provide.

## Radar–Camera Extrinsic Calibration
In contrast to the mature LiDAR–camera literature, automatic radar–camera calibration has received far less attention because commercial automotive radars produce very sparse point returns and virtually no reliable elevation information. Schöller et al. [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08743) proposed the first data-driven target-less method, restricted to the *rotational* component, using a coarse-to-fine CNN cascade for intelligent transportation systems. Cheng and Cao [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00787) later introduced an online target-less pipeline that exploits shared object-level features between radar and camera to estimate the extrinsic parameters. Both methods, however, either impose strong scene priors or ignore the intrinsic ambiguity of radar height, motivating the dual-perspective, height-aware formulation adopted here.

## Radar–Camera Fusion for 3D Perception
Because radar and camera share an increasingly popular sensor stack, a large body of work studies their *fusion* for 3D detection rather than their calibration, and many of these approaches inform the representations used in the present paper. CenterFusion associates radar returns to image-based object centers via frustum-based lookups [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.04841). CRAFT [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.06535) and TransCAR [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.00397) introduce transformer-style cross-attention between camera queries and radar tokens, while CramNet applies ray-constrained cross-attention in a shared 3D space to remain robust when one modality degrades [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.09267). Semantic alignment between the two views is exploited by MVFusion [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.10511). A parallel line of work performs fusion in bird's-eye view: CRN uses radar-assisted view transformation with deformable BEV cross-attention [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.00670); RCM-Fusion explicitly compensates radar's poor elevation via a radar-guided BEV encoder [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.10249); HVDetFusion offers a lightweight camera-radar detector [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.11323); LXL fuses 4D imaging radar with camera BEV features [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.00724); and RCBEVDet couples a dedicated RadarBEVNet with a cross-attention multi-layer fusion module [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.16440). CaFNet uses a confidence-driven scheme for radar-camera dense depth estimation [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.00697). These works consistently observe that frontal-view features preserve fine cross-modal cues but are highly sensitive to radar height noise, whereas BEV features are more robust but coarser—precisely the trade-off that the proposed Dual-Perspective representation and Selective Fusion Mechanism are designed to reconcile.

## Datasets for Radar-Based Perception
Empirical evaluation of radar–camera systems is anchored on large multimodal driving datasets. The nuScenes dataset [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/1903.11027) provides synchronized 6-camera, 5-radar, and LiDAR streams with dense 3D annotations and is used as the primary benchmark in this work. K-Radar [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.08171) further contributes 4D radar tensors and demonstrates radar's robustness under adverse weather, while RADIATE [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.09076) offers a scanning-radar dataset targeted at bad-weather perception, together justifying continued investment in radar-based automotive sensing.
