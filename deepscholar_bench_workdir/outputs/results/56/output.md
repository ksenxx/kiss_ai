# Related Work

## Sequential Recommendation

Sequential recommendation aims to predict a user's next item by modeling the temporal dependencies embedded in their historical behavior. Early work relied on recurrent networks to encode session-level dynamics, exemplified by GRU4Rec [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.06939), and later shifted toward self-attentive architectures such as SASRec [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.09781) and the bidirectional BERT4Rec [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.06690), which better captured long-range item transitions. Graph neural networks were also introduced to enrich sequential signals with high-order collaborative structure, as in MA-GNN [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.11730). To alleviate data sparsity and representation degeneration, a line of self-supervised and contrastive objectives has been proposed, including S³-Rec [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2008.07873), CL4SRec [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.14395), and DuoRec [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.05730). More recently, transferable text-based sequence encoders such as UniSRec [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.05941) and Recformer [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13731) have shown that semantic item descriptions can serve as a bridge across datasets and domains, motivating our LLM-based unified item representation.

## Cross-Domain (Sequential) Recommendation

Cross-domain recommendation transfers knowledge across item catalogs to mitigate sparsity in the target domain. Classical dual-network approaches, such as CoNet [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.06769), share hidden states across domains via cross connections, while unified graph-attention frameworks generalize dual-target and multi-target transfer [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.07976). Contrastive cross-domain matching, as in CCDR [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.00999), improves alignment with intra- and inter-domain contrastive tasks. In the sequential setting, cross-domain sequential recommendation (CDSR) additionally requires modeling mixed item transitions. MIFN augments transfer with mixed information flow and knowledge graphs [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.00485), DASL introduces dual attention for cross-domain CTR [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.02768), and DDGHM couples dual dynamic graphs with hybrid metric training [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.10163). C²DSR further exploits contrastive infomax across single- and cross-domain user views [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03891), and triple sequence learning models auxiliary sequences to distill transferable preferences [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.05027). Nevertheless, most of these methods depend heavily on *overlapping* users who interact across all domains, and struggle with the complex transition patterns of mixed sequences—two limitations that motivate our work. A very recent effort, MACD, targets open-world CDSR via model-agnostic contrastive denoising, but still operates on ID-based sequences without semantic bridging [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.04760).

## Large Language Models for Recommendation

The strong representation and reasoning abilities of LLMs have inspired a rapidly growing body of LLM-based recommenders. Generative paradigms cast recommendation as a language task: P5 unifies multiple recommendation objectives via personalized prompting [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.13366), GPT4Rec generates hypothetical items to retrieve candidates [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03879), and Chat-Rec turns interaction history into conversational prompts [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14524). Instruction-tuned recommenders, notably TALLRec [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.00447), PALR [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.07622), and bi-step grounding of LLMs to item spaces [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08434), adapt open-source LLMs to recommendation with lightweight tuning. To couple LLM semantics with collaborative signals, LLaRA hybridizes ID embeddings with textual prompts [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02445), E4SRec injects ID embeddings into an LLM for sequential prediction [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02443), CoLLM integrates collaborative embeddings into frozen LLM backbones [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.19488), and LatentReasoner interprets the latent space of sequential recommenders through LLMs [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.20487). Another line uses LLMs as auxiliary knowledge or profile generators rather than end-to-end predictors: KAR augments recommenders with open-world knowledge from LLMs [29](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.10933), ONCE combines open- and closed-source LLMs for content-based recommendation [30](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06566), LLMRec applies LLM-driven graph augmentation [31](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.00423), and RLMRec aligns LLM-based user/item profiles with collaborative representations [32](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.15950). Empirical studies have also probed whether LLMs genuinely understand user preferences [33](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06474) and revisited ID- versus modality-based recommenders in the LLM era [34](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.13835), while surveys consolidate this fast-moving field [35](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05817). Closest to our setting, LLMSeqSim-style approaches show that LLM item embeddings can directly power sequential recommendation [36](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.01339). However, none of these works explicitly tackles CDSR's *overlap dilemma* and *transition complexity*; our LLM4CDSR fills this gap by combining an LLM-based unified item representation with a contrastively regularized adapter and a hierarchical LLM profiling module inside a tri-thread cross-domain framework.
