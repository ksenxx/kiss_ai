# Related Work

Summarizing long-form narratives such as novels, movie scripts, and TV shows sits at the intersection of three active research threads: narrative-specific summarization, long-context modeling, and multi-agent LLM systems. We review each area and situate NexusSum within it.

## Narrative Summarization

A first line of work has focused on building resources and models tailored to story-like inputs. BookSum [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.08209) provides multi-granularity summaries of full books and chapters, while MovieSum [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.06281) and Select-and-Summarize (MENSA) [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.03561) target long screenplays that mix dialogue with stage directions. SummScreen [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.07091) focuses on abstractive summarization of TV transcripts, and NarraSum [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.01476) offers a large-scale corpus of general narrative summaries. These benchmarks all expose the same difficulties that motivate NexusSum: extremely long inputs, evolving character arcs, and the interleaving of prose and dialogue. Early modeling efforts adopt an extract-then-abstract paradigm, either selecting salient chapters or scenes [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.01840) or filtering by scene saliency classifiers [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.03561), but these pipelines routinely drop character interactions that are critical for coherence. Dialogue-heavy inputs have also inspired preprocessing tricks such as speaker normalization and bag-of-tricks strategies [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.08232), which anticipate our Dialogue-to-Description Transformation but do not scale to book-length narratives.

## Long-Context Summarization

A second line of work modifies transformer architectures so that a single model can ingest tens of thousands of tokens. Sparse-attention models such as Longformer [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.05150), Big Bird [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.14062), Reformer [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.04451), and Linformer [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.04768) reduce the quadratic cost of self-attention, while LongT5 [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.07916) combines transient global attention with denoising pre-training for long-input generation. Positional-interpolation techniques [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.15595) further extend the effective context of pretrained LLMs. However, empirical studies show that even models with 100K+ token contexts suffer from the "lost in the middle" phenomenon and degrade sharply on multi-turn dependencies, entity tracking, and thematic continuity [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172). Chunk-based methods sidestep hard length limits by segmenting the document: SLED [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.00748) fuses short-encoder passes over overlapping windows, Unlimiformer [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.01625) augments a transformer decoder with an unbounded retrieval index over encoder states, and CachED [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.01805) uses gradient caching for encoder-decoder fine-tuning. Multi-stage extractive-abstractive frameworks such as SummN generalize this idea by recursively summarizing coarse-to-fine partitions. All of these approaches require fine-tuning or architectural modifications, whereas NexusSum operates in a training-free regime on top of off-the-shelf LLMs.

## Multi-Agent LLMs for Long-Form Summarization

A third and more recent line of work coordinates multiple LLM calls as cooperating agents. A survey of LLM-based multi-agent systems [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.01680) documents the rapid diversification of role-based, hierarchical, and refinement pipelines. Directly relevant to summarization, BooookScore [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.00785) systematically studies hierarchical merging and incremental updating for book-length inputs, showing that chunked LLM pipelines can rival fine-tuned specialists on coherence metrics. Chain of Agents [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.02818) formalizes a multi-agent pipeline in which each worker refines a rolling summary passed sequentially along the chunks. Agent-as-Judge frameworks combine hierarchical summarization with factual refinement over character knowledge graphs [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.09993). Beyond pure summarization, retrieval-augmented generation [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401) improves factuality by grounding outputs in retrieved passages, though it struggles with the thematic continuity required for storytelling. These systems demonstrate the promise of coordinating specialized LLM agents but treat documents largely as generic text; they do not exploit narrative-specific structure such as dialogue attribution, scene boundaries, or controllable output length.

## Positioning of NexusSum

Complementing prior evidence that modern zero-shot LLMs still trail task-specific pipelines on abstractive summarization benchmarks [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.09558), NexusSum bridges the three threads above. Like narrative-summarization work, it explicitly models the prose/dialogue duality via a Dialogue-to-Description Transformation. Like long-context methods, it segments arbitrarily long inputs into scene-based chunks, but avoids architectural changes or fine-tuning. Like multi-agent frameworks such as Chain of Agents [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.02818), BooookScore [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.00785), and hierarchical merge-and-refine agents [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.09993), it decomposes summarization into cooperating LLM roles, but adds an Iterative Compression agent that provides fine-grained length control—an aspect largely absent from prior multi-agent summarization pipelines. Together these choices yield state-of-the-art BERTScore on BookSum [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.08209), MovieSum [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.06281), MENSA [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.03561), and SummScreen [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.07091), while remaining fine-tuning-free.
