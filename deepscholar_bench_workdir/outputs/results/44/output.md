# Related Work

## Collaborative Filtering with Graph Neural Networks

Modern collaborative filtering (CF) is dominated by embedding-based user–item matching, from classical matrix factorization trained with pairwise ranking losses such as BPR [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1205.2618) to deep neural interaction models like NCF [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1708.05031). More recently, graph neural networks (GNNs) have become the de-facto backbone for CF by explicitly propagating collaborative signals along the user–item bipartite graph. GC-MC formulates matrix completion as link prediction on this graph [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.02263), while inductive GNN frameworks such as GraphSAGE [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.02216) and its industrial-scale variant PinSage [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1806.01973) enable neighborhood aggregation over billions of nodes. NGCF injects high-order connectivity into embeddings via multi-layer message passing [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.08108), and LightGCN subsequently shows that removing nonlinear transformations yields a simpler yet stronger CF backbone [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.02126). Building on this line, UltraGCN approximates the limit of infinite-layer propagation with constraint losses for efficiency [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.15114), DGCF disentangles latent user intents [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.01764), KGAT extends attention-based aggregation to knowledge graphs [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.07854), and SimpleX demonstrates a strong non-graph baseline with cosine contrastive loss [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.12613). Contrastive and self-supervised objectives have further boosted CF performance: SGL augments interaction graphs through node/edge dropout [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.10783), NCL leverages structural and semantic neighbors as contrastive views [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.06200), SimGCL adds simple embedding-level noise instead of graph augmentation [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.08679), and HCCF extends contrastive learning to hypergraph structures [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.12200). Our work adopts a lightweight GNN encoder in the spirit of LightGCN [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.02126) but shifts the focus from full-precision accuracy to compact binary representations.

## Representation Binarization and Hashing for Retrieval

Compressing continuous embeddings into compact binary codes is a well-studied route to memory- and latency-efficient inference. In computer vision and general deep learning, Binarized Neural Networks constrain weights and activations to ±1 during both training and inference [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.02830), and XNOR-Net accelerates convolutions with bitwise XNOR and popcount operations [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1603.05279). Deep-to-hash approaches such as HashNet learn binary codes through a continuation strategy that gradually anneals the sign non-linearity [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.00758). In the recommendation domain, product-quantization–based methods compress embedding tables in a differentiable end-to-end manner [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.09756), while candidate generation with binary codes explores Hamming-space retrieval for large-scale top-N recommendation [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.05475). The predecessor of the present work, BiGeaR, introduces multi-faceted quantization reinforcement at the pre-, mid-, and post-stages of graph representation binarization and achieves substantial improvements over prior quantization-based recommenders [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.02115). However, existing quantization/hashing approaches primarily minimize numerical distortion and largely overlook the information loss introduced by binarization itself, which motivates our fine-grained inference-level treatment.

## Knowledge Distillation and Data Augmentation

To mitigate the information loss induced by discretization, we borrow ideas from knowledge distillation and augmentation. The classical KD framework transfers dark-knowledge from a teacher to a compact student via softened logits [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/1503.02531). Ranking Distillation adapts this idea to recommendation by having a compact student mimic the top-ranked items of a heavy teacher [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.07428), while DE-RRD jointly distills latent knowledge and relaxed rankings for recommender systems [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.04357). Orthogonally, mixup-style linear interpolation of samples and labels [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/1710.09412) has inspired synthetic sample generation in a variety of representation learning settings. BiGeaR++ builds on both strands by (i) introducing a fine-grained inference distillation mechanism that transfers rank-aware signals from the full-precision teacher to the binarized student and (ii) synthesizing pseudo-positive samples from both real items and latent embeddings, thereby explicitly counteracting the information loss that pure numerical quantization [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.02830), [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1603.05279), [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.09756), [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.02115) fails to address.
