# Related Work

## Conversational Recommender Systems

Conversational recommender systems (CRSs) aim to elicit user preferences through multi-turn interaction and provide personalized recommendations. Early "attribute-centric" CRSs cast preference elicitation as an interactive decision problem in which the system alternates between asking about attributes and recommending items. Representative approaches such as the Estimation–Action–Reflection (EAR) framework [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.09102) and the Simple Conversational Path Reasoning (SCPR) model over knowledge graphs [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00194) coordinate a question-recommendation policy with a factorization-based recommender. In parallel, "generation-oriented" CRSs, seeded by the ReDial dataset and dialogue system [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.07617), focus on producing natural conversational responses that also mention appropriate items. Follow-up work has introduced external knowledge to strengthen preference modeling and response generation: KBRD injects a knowledge graph into both the recommender and the dialogue module [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.05391); KGSF fuses word-oriented and entity-oriented knowledge graphs via mutual information maximization [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.04032); and CR-Walker performs tree-structured reasoning over a knowledge graph coupled with explicit dialog acts [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.10333). UniCRS further unifies the recommendation and generation subtasks through knowledge-enhanced prompt learning on a frozen language model [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.09363).

## LLMs as Conversational Recommenders

The rise of large language models (LLMs) has reshaped CRS research. Chat-REC augments a classical recommender with ChatGPT to enable interactive and explainable recommendations [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14524), while a comprehensive study shows that LLMs can act as strong zero-shot conversational recommenders even without domain-specific fine-tuning [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.10053). Agent-style frameworks like InteRecAgent [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.16505) and RecMind [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.14296) equip LLMs with tools, memory, and planning to orchestrate multi-step interactive recommendation. Complementary studies explore refining LLM-based recommendations via feedback-driven reprompting [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.03605). To make LLMs recommendation-aware, instruction-tuning methods such as InstructRec cast recommendation as instruction following [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.07001), and TALLRec efficiently aligns LLMs with recommendation objectives [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.00447). Our simulated user follows a similar instruction-tuning paradigm but targets the *user side* of the conversation rather than the system side.

## User Simulators for CRS Evaluation and Training

Because collecting real conversational interactions is costly, simulated users have become an important tool for both evaluating and training CRSs. iEvaLM highlights that interactive evaluation with LLM-based user simulators reveals capabilities of CRSs that static, turn-level metrics miss, and calls for more faithful simulators [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13112). Broader efforts model user behavior with LLM-based agents to reproduce plausible interaction trajectories in recommendation environments [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.02552). Our generative reward model based simulated user (GRSU) extends this line by explicitly designing coarse- and fine-grained feedback actions that mirror how a reward model scores and critiques candidate items.

## Generative Reward Models and Reward-Guided Search

Our design is inspired by advances in reward modeling and reward-guided reasoning. RLHF-style training aligns LLMs with human preferences via a scalar reward model learned from comparisons [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02155), and Direct Preference Optimization reformulates preference learning without an explicit reward model [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18290). Recent work reformulates reward models themselves as *generative* verifiers that produce natural-language judgments or scores via next-token prediction, improving verification quality and transferability [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.15240). Process- and step-level reward models have proven effective for guiding multi-step reasoning: "Let's Verify Step by Step" shows that process supervision beats outcome supervision on math reasoning [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.20050), and Math-Shepherd trains such verifiers without human annotations to reinforce LLM reasoning [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.08935). These verifiers are typically deployed with search-based inference procedures such as Tree of Thoughts [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.10601) or, more generally, test-time compute scaling with best-of-N and beam search [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.03314). Analogously, we treat the simulated user's generative scores and attribute-based critiques as a reward signal and adopt beam search over CRS-generated candidates, balancing effectiveness and efficiency, and further introducing an efficient candidate ranking step to consolidate the recommendations derived from the interaction.
