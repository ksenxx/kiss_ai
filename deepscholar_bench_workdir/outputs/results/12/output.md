## Related Work

**Event cameras and asynchronous vision.** Event cameras are bio-inspired sensors that report per-pixel brightness changes asynchronously, offering microsecond latency, high dynamic range, and sparse output. A comprehensive overview of the sensing principle, algorithmic paradigms, and application domains is given by the survey of Gallego et al. [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08405). Because the raw event stream is asynchronous and irregular, a substantial body of work has focused on converting events into intermediate representations suitable for deep networks. Gehrig et al. propose an end-to-end learned Event Spike Tensor that unifies many hand-crafted grid-based representations under a single differentiable framework [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08245), while Bi et al. formulate the event stream as a spatio-temporal graph and use Graph Neural Networks for neuromorphic object classification [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.06648). To retain the native sparsity of the sensor, Messikommer et al. introduce asynchronous sparse convolutional networks that only update outputs where new events occur [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.09148), and Schaefer et al. extend this idea with AEGNN, an asynchronous event-based graph neural network that processes events incrementally without recomputing the full graph [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.17149). Our probabilistic framework is complementary to these representations: rather than modifying the downstream architecture, we reduce the number of events fed into any existing pipeline.

**Event downsampling and subsampling.** The high firing rate of event cameras motivates methods that reduce the event volume before further processing. Early work by Cohen et al. explored spatial and temporal downsampling for event-based classification, while several later approaches rely on fixed heuristics or thresholding rules; these have known limitations in adaptability, as highlighted in our discussion. Recent learning-oriented efforts push the boundaries of subsampling: Araghi et al. systematically study how far events can be dropped while preserving CNN video classification accuracy [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.08953), showing that dense-frame CNNs remain surprisingly robust down to very low event budgets but that the choice of *which* events to keep matters critically. In contrast to their offline, model-specific selection, POLED estimates an event-importance probability density function *online* from the raw stream and is designed to remain useful in a zero-shot manner across downstream tasks. Our contour-preserving ePDF is also related in spirit to bio-inspired filtering rules, but replaces hand-tuned thresholds with a probabilistic acceptance mechanism whose target distribution can be arbitrarily specified.

**Event-based detection, interpolation and dense prediction benchmarks.** POLED is evaluated on several downstream tasks that have their own dedicated literature. For automotive object detection, the large-scale Gen1 dataset introduced by de Tournemire et al. [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08499) and the 1-Megapixel event detection dataset of Perot et al. [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.13436) have become standard benchmarks. On the modeling side, Recurrent Vision Transformers (RVT) by Gehrig and Scaramuzza set a strong baseline for event-based detection by combining local self-attention with recurrent temporal aggregation [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.05598); we use such trained models to test whether their accuracy is preserved when the input stream is aggressively subsampled by our ePDF, without any retraining. For high-temporal-resolution video frame interpolation, TimeLens by Tulyakov et al. fuses event and frame information to synthesize intermediate frames at microsecond scale [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.07286). Finally, dense per-pixel prediction from events has been explored in event-based shape-from-polarization by Muglikar et al., who show that events can recover surface normals under polarized illumination [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.06855). These tasks span classification, interpolation, dense regression, and detection, and provide a stringent test bed for evaluating whether *task-agnostic* event downsampling can retain performance under strict event budgets.

**Positioning.** In summary, existing event downsampling schemes are largely heuristic and task-specific, while recent representation-learning and asynchronous-processing approaches [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08245),[4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.09148),[5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.17149) focus on efficient consumption rather than principled reduction of the raw stream. POLED sits between these lines of work: it is a purely online, probabilistic sampler that operates before any downstream model, is compatible with modern detectors, interpolators, and dense predictors [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.05598),[10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.07286),[11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.06855), and, unlike prior subsampling studies [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.08953), is explicitly evaluated in a zero-shot setting on datasets ranging from N-Caltech-style classification to automotive detection benchmarks [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08499),[8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.13436).
