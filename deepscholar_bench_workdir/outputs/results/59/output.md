# Related Work

## Fairness Notions and Metrics in Ranking

The study of fairness in ranking has produced a variety of criteria, including group parity in top-*k* results, exposure-based fairness, and individual fairness. FA*IR introduced a statistically grounded post-processing scheme that guarantees a minimum proportion of protected items in every prefix of a ranking [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.06368). Building on this, exposure-based formulations argue that attention decays with position and that fairness should be measured with respect to allocated exposure rather than raw slot counts [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07281). Individual fairness has been formalized as *equity of attention*, which amortizes attention across queries so that items of comparable relevance accumulate comparable exposure over time [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1805.01788), and as optimal-transport-based individual fairness for learning-to-rank [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.11023). Two comprehensive surveys catalogue the resulting landscape of metrics and algorithms and highlight the fragmentation of evaluation practice [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.14000), [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.03761). Standardized evaluation efforts such as the TREC Fair Ranking track [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.11650) and studies of author-gender exposure in book recommendation [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.07586) further illustrate that a single fairness statistic rarely captures the full behavior of a system—an observation that directly motivates our Elastic Fairness Curve.

## Algorithms for Fair Re-ranking

A large body of work re-ranks candidate lists to satisfy fairness constraints while retaining as much accuracy as possible. Score transformation and re-ranking pipelines such as those deployed at LinkedIn provide attribute-aware constraints at production scale [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.01989), and the FairSearch toolkit packages FA*IR and DELTR for practitioners [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.13134). Optimization-based approaches formulate fair exposure as a linear program over doubly-stochastic ranking policies [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07281), while policy-gradient methods extend this to end-to-end learning-to-rank with fairness regularizers [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1902.04056), pairwise fairness constraints [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/1903.00780), disparate-exposure penalties (DELTR) [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/1805.08716), and differentiable ranking layers [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.10723). Feedback-loop settings introduce proportional controllers (FairCo) that dynamically enforce amortized merit-based exposure [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14713), while diversity-oriented graph algorithms such as FairMatch improve aggregate coverage without heavy accuracy losses [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.01148). Personalized re-ranking transformers illustrate how contextual list-wise models can be adapted for fairness objectives [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.06813). More recent work computes Pareto-optimal amortizations of exposure under DBN click models [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.07647), and probabilistic frameworks address protected-attribute uncertainty during fair ranking [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.02091).

## Two-sided and Multi-stakeholder Fairness

Recommendation platforms serve both consumers and producers, so re-ranking must balance user utility against item- or provider-side exposure. Beyond-parity objectives were among the first to formalize this tension for collaborative filtering [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.08804). Popularity-bias analyses show that head items systematically dominate exposure and marginalize long-tail providers [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1907.13286). Two-sided formulations such as FairRec guarantee envy-free exposure for producers while respecting consumer preferences [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.10764), TFROM jointly re-ranks for customer and provider fairness [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.09024), and CPFair unifies consumer and producer fairness in a single re-ranking objective [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.08085). Max-min provider fairness has been tackled with online mirror-descent re-ranking (P-MMF) [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.06660), and Lorenz-dominance criteria characterize the whole family of two-sided welfare trade-offs [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.15781). User-oriented perspectives quantify how re-ranking disparately affects different consumer groups [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.10671), and knowledge-graph explanations have been leveraged to inject fairness signals into path-based recommenders [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.02046).

## Accuracy–Fairness Trade-off and Positioning of This Work

Almost every method above reports an accuracy–fairness trade-off, yet comparisons typically rely on a single point estimate of one fairness metric, obscuring how algorithms behave when the strength of the fairness constraint varies. Surveys explicitly identify this as a limitation of current evaluation methodology [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.14000), [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.03761), and two-sided welfare studies motivate curve-based reporting via dominance relations [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.15781). Our economic analogy—treating item-side fairness as a tax whose incidence depends on the *elasticity* of utility between item groups—unifies these observations, and the proposed EF-Curve and ElasticRank complement prior algorithms [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.06368), [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07281), [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.10764), [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.08085), [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.06660) by exposing the full trade-off frontier rather than a single operating point.
