# Related Works

Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) has attracted sustained attention because inner-product ranking underpins large-scale recommendation, retrieval, and dense-vector language models. Existing solutions can be grouped along two orthogonal axes: (i) the geometric assumption they make about the underlying similarity (raw inner product vs. an isomorphic Euclidean space) and (ii) the algorithmic family they belong to (hashing, quantization, or graph traversal). Our Metric-Amphibious Graph (MAG) bridges these axes, and we position it against the following bodies of work.

**Reductions from MIPS to Euclidean NNS.** A prolific line of work reduces MIPS to nearest-neighbor search under the Euclidean or angular metric by lifting vectors into an augmented space. The Asymmetric LSH (ALSH) framework [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1405.5869) established the first sublinear MIPS scheme through such a transformation, later refined by improved ALSH constructions [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1410.5410) and by Neyshabur and Srebro who showed that a suitably chosen symmetric LSH can also solve MIPS [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1410.5518). Follow-up analyses generalize the transformation to hybrid spaces mixing inner products and distances [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1903.08690). While elegant, all such reductions inflate dimensionality and distort local topology — the "topology destruction" issue our paper studies — motivating a search strategy that does not rely on a fixed projection.

**Quantization-based MIPS.** A second family avoids space projections and instead compresses vectors to accelerate inner-product evaluation. Anisotropic vector quantization (ScaNN) tailors codebooks to the MIPS loss and delivers strong recall/throughput trade-offs [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.10396), and norm-explicit quantization further decouples direction from magnitude to reduce quantization error under IP similarity [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.04654). These techniques are complementary to graph indexes and are frequently combined with them in billion-scale systems [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.08734).

**Graph-based ANN indexes.** Proximity graphs have become the dominant paradigm for approximate nearest-neighbor search under Euclidean or angular metrics. Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs (HNSW) [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1603.09320) and the Navigating Spreading-out Graph (NSG) [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.00143) are the canonical representatives, and a comprehensive experimental survey documents their dominance [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.12631). Extensions target billion-scale storage hierarchies [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.08566), streaming updates [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.09613), inverted-index hybrids [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.02422), learned routing to accelerate traversal [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.10987), and low-precision distance approximations that cut per-hop cost [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.11408). MAG inherits the graph paradigm but adapts its construction and navigation directly to MIPS rather than Euclidean NNS.

**Graph-based MIPS.** Extending proximity graphs to non-metric inner-product similarity is nontrivial because IP violates the triangle inequality and the identity of indiscernibles. Analytical work on inner-product NSW graphs uncovered a strong norm bias in graph traversal and proposed norm-adjusted heuristics that alleviate it [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.13459). Recent randomized samplers such as BanditMIPS give complementary theoretical speedups whose runtime is independent of vector dimension [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.07551). Our work argues that the IP and Euclidean paradigms need not be exclusive: MAG interleaves both during indexing and search, a design that departs from prior single-metric graph indexes.

**MIPS variants and benchmarks.** Beyond top-$k$ MIPS, reverse MIPS [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.07131), shifting-aware reverse-$k$ MIPS via asymmetric hashing [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.12751), and diversity-aware $k$-MIPS [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.13858) capture recommendation-oriented queries. Standardized evaluations such as ANN-Benchmarks [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1807.05614), high-dimensional experimental surveys [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.02455), and reviews of hashing algorithms [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.07545) provide the empirical yardsticks against which our 12-dataset study of MAG is calibrated.
