# Related Work

## Sequential Recommendation

Sequential recommendation models a user's historical interactions as an ordered sequence and predicts the next item. Early neural approaches used recurrent networks such as GRU4Rec [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.06939) to summarize session behavior, and convolutional architectures such as Caser [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.07426) that treat recent items as an "image" to capture point-, union-, and skip-level patterns. The Transformer [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) later became the dominant backbone: SASRec [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.09781) uses a causal self-attention encoder to weigh past items adaptively, while BERT4Rec [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.06690) extends this idea with bidirectional masked-item modeling. Follow-up work has enriched the input side of self-attention (e.g., S³-Rec, which pre-trains with mutual-information maximization over items, attributes, and segments [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2008.07873)), or improved its objective via contrastive learning, as in CL4SRec [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.14395) and DuoRec [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.05730). Efficiency-oriented variants such as LinRec [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.01537) explore linear-attention alternatives for long sequences. Like our work, these methods build on the self-attention transformer; unlike ours, they focus on a single domain and do not address cross-domain knowledge transfer inside the attention module itself.

## Cross-Domain (Sequential) Recommendation

Cross-domain recommendation (CDR) transfers signals from data-rich source domains to alleviate sparsity in a target domain. CoNet couples two feed-forward networks through learned cross connections [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.06769); MiNet blends long- and short-term user interests across domains for CTR prediction [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2008.02974); DDTCDR performs deep dual transfer with orthogonal mappings [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.05189); PTUPCDR learns personalized preference bridges to combat the well-documented negative-transfer problem [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.11154); and CDRIB uses a variational information bottleneck to disentangle transferable and domain-specific signals for cold-start users [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.16863). Contrastive and adversarial CDR variants — CCDR [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.00999) and RecGURU [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.10093) — align user representations across domains through auxiliary self-supervised objectives.

Cross-domain sequential recommendation (CDSR) combines these two threads. MIFN routes mixed information flows across knowledge-graph–enhanced parallel sequences [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.00485). DASL introduces dual attentive sequential learning to share user history across domains for CTR prediction [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.02768). C2DSR augments a cross-domain sequential encoder with contrastive infomax objectives on single- and joint-domain representations [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03891). More recently, CGRec frames CDSR as a cooperative game and computes Shapley-style contributions to identify and down-weight domains that induce negative transfer [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.13188). While effective, most of these designs *bolt on* extra domain-aware components (parallel sub-networks, dual attention blocks, contrastive heads, or game-theoretic weightings) around a vanilla transformer. Our work is complementary: rather than adding new modules, we directly reshape the self-attention weights inside the backbone so that cross-domain interactions are learned in a Pareto-optimal fashion.

## Multi-Objective and Pareto Optimization for Learning

Because CDSR must simultaneously fit the target-domain task and control cross-domain influence, we cast it as a multi-objective problem. Kendall et al. show that uncertainty-based loss weighting can adaptively trade off related objectives [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.07115); GradNorm rebalances tasks by normalizing gradient magnitudes [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.02257); PCGrad removes destructive gradient interference by projecting conflicting gradients [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.06782); and CAGrad searches directions that minimize a worst-case objective for conflict-averse updates [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.14048). More formally, Sener and Koltun view multi-task learning as multi-objective optimization and use the multiple-gradient descent algorithm to converge to Pareto-stationary solutions [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04650), while Lin et al. propose Pareto Multi-Task Learning to explore an entire front of task trade-offs [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.12854). AutoCDSR draws inspiration from this line of work, but instead of balancing task losses it applies Pareto-style optimization *inside* the attention mechanism, dynamically minimizing cross-domain attention scores while preserving in-domain recommendation quality. This positions our method as a lightweight, plug-and-play enhancement of the self-attention transformer that unifies ideas from sequential recommendation [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.09781)[5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.06690), cross-domain transfer [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.11154)[20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.13188), and multi-objective optimization [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04650)[26](https://arxiv.org/abs/1912.12854).
