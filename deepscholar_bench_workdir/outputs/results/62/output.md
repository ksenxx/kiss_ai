# Related Work

**Neural and Dense Retrieval.** Modern information retrieval has been dominated by dual-encoder and late-interaction architectures that embed queries and documents into a shared vector space and retrieve via approximate nearest neighbor search. Representative approaches such as DPR [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.04906), ColBERT [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.12832), and ANCE [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00808) established strong baselines on open-domain question answering and passage retrieval. Benchmarks such as BEIR [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663) later revealed that these models often struggle when transferred to out-of-distribution corpora, motivating work on more corpus-adaptive paradigms.

**Generative Retrieval.** Generative retrieval reformulates search as an autoregressive sequence generation problem, in which a Transformer [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) trained with sequence-to-sequence objectives (T5 [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.10683), BART [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.13461)) directly emits document identifiers. GENRE first applied this idea to entity retrieval by generating entity names under a prefix-trie constraint [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.00904). The Differentiable Search Index (DSI) generalized the paradigm by mapping documents to structured integer IDs learned end-to-end [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.06991), and the Neural Corpus Indexer (NCI) refined the identifier structure with hierarchical clustering and query generation [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.02743). SEAL extends the identifier space to arbitrary n-gram substrings using an FM-index for constrained decoding [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.10628), while Ultron [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.09257) and CorpusBrain [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.07652) introduce model-based indexers and knowledge-intensive pre-training. Subsequent work has explored richer identifiers and training signals: GenRet learns discrete document tokens [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.04171), TSGen uses unordered term sets as identifiers [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13859), TIGER applies semantic IDs to generative recommendation [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.05065), a unified prompt-based generative retriever tackles multiple knowledge-intensive tasks [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.14856), and listwise sequential learning further improves ranking quality [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.12499). DSI-QG bridges indexing and retrieval with synthetic queries [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.10128), and recent analysis has shown that generative retrieval can be interpreted as a form of multi-vector dense retrieval [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.00684).

**Scaling and Out-of-Distribution Generalization.** As generative retrieval matured, several empirical studies probed its limits. Pradeep et al. examined scaling behavior to millions of passages and reported that many DSI-style design choices fail to transfer to large corpora [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.11841). Liu et al. benchmarked generative retrievers under distribution shift and observed pronounced robustness gaps against dense baselines [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.12756). Nonparametric decoding partially mitigates these issues by grounding generation in contextualized document embeddings [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.02068). Yet a principled theoretical account of why constrained autoregressive decoding generalizes poorly to new corpora has remained missing—precisely the gap our analysis targets.

**Constrained Decoding and Beam Search.** Because generative retrieval relies on corpus-specific constraints and beam search, its behavior is intimately tied to well-known pathologies of autoregressive generation. NeuroLogic decoding [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.12884) and constrained NLG methods [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.07220) show that hard lexical constraints can distort the underlying distribution. In machine translation, beam search has been shown to expose severe model errors: exact inference frequently prefers empty outputs [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.10090), length bias and the "beam-search curse" degrade quality as beams widen [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.10006), and larger beams can even hurt performance [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03872). Meister et al. connect beam search to a regularized decoding objective, arguing that the algorithm succeeds despite—not because of—its approximation quality [29](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.02650), while Holtzman et al. document how likelihood-maximizing decoding produces degenerate text [30](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.09751). Our work extends these insights to the retrieval setting, formally characterizing how corpus-specific constraints and marginal-based beam scoring introduce irreducible error in the Bayes-optimal generative retriever.
