# Related Works

**In-Context Learning and Demonstration Selection.** Since large language models were shown to perform tasks from only a handful of in-context demonstrations [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165), a large body of work has investigated how the choice of exemplars shapes downstream accuracy [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.00234). Retrieval-based selection was popularized by [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.06804), which picks semantically similar training examples for each test query, and later extended by learned retrievers that are optimized directly for prompt utility [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.08633). Complementary lines of work reduce the annotation budget by choosing a diverse, informative subset to label a priori [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.01975), select prompts via reinforcement learning [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.04486), or compress the corpus from an information-theoretic viewpoint [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.10375). Beyond content, prompt *ordering* itself dramatically affects performance [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08786), and complementary explanations have been shown to further boost few-shot reasoning [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.13892). All these approaches implicitly assume that the underlying demonstration pool is clean — an assumption that our work explicitly relaxes.

**Role of Labels and Robustness of ICL.** A separate strand of research asks *what* aspects of demonstrations drive ICL. [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.12837) argued that models often exploit format and label-space cues rather than exact input–label correspondence, whereas [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.12685) provided a finer-grained analysis showing that ground-truth labels do matter for many tasks and models. Studies of demonstration robustness under limited or perturbed data further confirm that ICL is sensitive to noisy annotations [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.10693), motivating dedicated methods for handling label corruption in the prompt itself [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.19581). Related few-shot recipes such as LM-BFF [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.15723) and noisy-channel prompting [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.04106) improve stability but do not target explicit noise detection.

**Learning from Noisy Labels.** Robustness to noisy annotations has been studied extensively in classical supervised learning. Neural networks memorize even random labels [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.03530), yet remain surprisingly robust to high noise ratios given sufficient clean signal [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.10694). Practical remedies include Co-teaching between paired networks [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.06872), confident learning that estimates joint label distributions [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.00068), example-forgetting statistics [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.05159), and influence-based data cleansing for SGD [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.08473); see [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.08199) for a survey. In NLP, pretrained language models themselves have been leveraged to detect label errors via loss- or perplexity-based scoring [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.12702), and external LLM guidance has been used to filter noisy fine-tuning data [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.01108). These approaches typically rank samples by *local* likelihood, so — as we demonstrate — their performance degrades sharply once the noise ratio grows large enough to shift the reference distribution.

**Perplexity, Calibration, and Debiasing in LLMs.** Perplexity is the workhorse metric for scoring generations, but recent work shows it is an unreliable proxy for text quality [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.05892) and is systematically biased by prompt format, majority labels, and domain priors baked into the model [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.09690). To counteract such biases, contrastive decoding subtracts an amateur-model log-likelihood from an expert's [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.15097), and DoLa contrasts predictions across transformer layers to reduce hallucination [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.03883). Our dual-debiasing framework is inspired by these ideas: rather than trusting raw perplexity, we synthesize neighboring samples to disentangle annotation bias from the model's intrinsic domain-knowledge bias, yielding a Sample Cleanliness Score that remains reliable even when the corpus is dominated by noise.
