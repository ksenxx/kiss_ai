# Related Work

## Classical and Neural Collaborative Filtering
Collaborative filtering (CF) has traditionally relied on latent-factor models that learn low-dimensional embeddings from user–item interactions. Bayesian Personalized Ranking [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1205.2618) introduced the pairwise ranking objective that has become a standard training signal for implicit-feedback CF, while Neural Collaborative Filtering [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1708.05031) generalized inner-product matrix factorization by replacing it with multi-layer perceptrons over user and item embeddings. Despite their success, these approaches treat interactions as isolated points and cannot directly exploit the higher-order connectivity that emerges in the user–item bipartite graph.

## Graph-based Collaborative Filtering
To capture such connectivity, graph convolutional networks (GCNs) have been widely adopted. GC-MC [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.02263) frames CF as link prediction on a bipartite graph via a graph auto-encoder, and PinSage [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1806.01973) scales this idea to web-scale item graphs using neighborhood sampling. NGCF [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.08108) explicitly propagates embeddings through multiple hops to inject high-order collaborative signals, and LightGCN [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.02126) simplifies NGCF by discarding feature transformation and non-linearities, showing that linear neighborhood aggregation alone yields the largest gains. Follow-up works pursue further simplification and robustness: LR-GCCF [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.10167) adds residual linear propagation, and UltraGCN [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.15114) approximates infinite-layer propagation with a constraint-based loss. Self-supervised extensions such as SGL [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.10783) and SimGCL [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.08679) improve representation quality through graph or embedding-level contrastive augmentations. Nevertheless, all of these methods depend on a learned embedding layer whose latent dimension inherently bounds the spectral capacity of the model, and their neighborhood aggregation is restricted to a low-order linear polynomial of the adjacency matrix.

## Spectral Graph Neural Networks
Our work is grounded in spectral graph theory, following a line of research that formulates graph convolutions as filtering in the graph Fourier domain. ChebNet [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.09375) approximates spectral filters with Chebyshev polynomials of the Laplacian to obtain fast, localized convolutions, and the widely-used GCN [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/1609.02907) reduces this to a first-order low-pass filter. SGC [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/1902.07153) further removes non-linearities, showing that repeated smoothing already captures much of the signal. More expressive spectral designs have since been proposed: GPR-GNN [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.07988) learns generalized PageRank coefficients that adapt across the frequency spectrum, BernNet [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.10994) parameterizes arbitrary filters via Bernstein basis, and ChebNetII [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.03580) revisits Chebyshev approximation with interpolation at Chebyshev nodes to overcome the Runge phenomenon and negative-response issues of the original ChebNet. Related analyses [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1906.02174) argue that flexible multi-scale filters mitigate over-smoothing that plagues stacked low-pass GCNs. Our ChebyCF adopts Chebyshev interpolation in the CF setting to realize a non-linear, spectrum-wide filter rather than a truncated low-pass one.

## Spectral and Filter-based Recommendation
A parallel thread applies spectral filtering directly to the user–item interaction matrix. SpectralCF [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.10523) performs convolution in the graph spectral domain to alleviate cold start. GF-CF [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.07567) shows analytically that many GCN-based recommenders — including LightGCN — reduce to low-pass graph filters, and proposes a closed-form filter that is competitive with deep models. BSPM [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.09324) extends this view by casting recommendation as a blurring–sharpening process governed by differential equations over the interaction signal. These training-free, embedding-free filters demonstrate that raw interaction histories carry rich signal, motivating our design of ChebyCF, which retains the raw interaction input, approximates a flexible non-linear filter via Chebyshev interpolation, and augments it with an ideal-pass component and degree-based normalization to overcome the frequency cut-off and linear-form limitations identified in prior GCN-based CF.
