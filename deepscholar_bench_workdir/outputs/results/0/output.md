# Related Work

## AI‑Driven Optimal Taxation and Fiscal Policy Design

A first line of work directly targeting adaptive tax‑policy design uses deep reinforcement learning (RL) to co‑train workers and a social planner in an economic simulator. The AI Economist [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.13332) proposes a two‑level deep RL framework in which heterogeneous worker agents and a tax‑setting planner learn concurrently, achieving a ~16% improvement over baselines—including the Saez optimal‑taxation framework—on the equality–productivity trade‑off, while remaining robust to emergent tax‑gaming strategies. Its extended version [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.02755) further formalizes the two‑level RL training procedure and demonstrates that learned tax schedules recover theoretically optimal policies in stylized one‑step economies and outperform them in dynamic multi‑period settings. TaxAI [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.16307) complements these efforts by building a Bewley–Aiyagari‑style dynamic macroeconomic simulator with up to 10,000 heterogeneous households and benchmarking a range of multi‑agent RL algorithms for tax‑policy learning, providing an open testbed for comparing planner strategies. Although these RL‑based approaches address the adaptability limitations of static systems such as the U.S. federal income tax, they still rely on hand‑crafted utility functions and rationality assumptions that our LLM‑based TaxAgent aims to relax.

## LLM‑Powered Generative Agents and Agent‑Based Modeling

A parallel thread reimagines agent‑based modeling (ABM) using large language models to endow agents with richer, more human‑like reasoning. Generative Agents [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442) introduce an LLM‑driven architecture combining memory, reflection, and planning to produce believable individual and emergent social behaviors in a sandbox town, establishing the design pattern that many subsequent economic and policy simulators build upon. Surveys of LLM‑based autonomous agents [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.11432) and of LLM‑empowered ABM specifically [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.11970) organize the design space along dimensions such as perception, memory, action generation, and human alignment, motivating our use of LLMs to capture taxpayer heterogeneity and boundedly rational behavior. Concordia [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.03664) further provides a library for constructing generative agent‑based models grounded in physical, social, or digital environments through a Game‑Master architecture, offering an infrastructure blueprint that inspired our macroeconomic simulator. Multi‑agent frameworks such as AgentVerse [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.10848) show that dynamically composed collectives of LLM agents can outperform single agents and give rise to non‑trivial emergent social dynamics, reinforcing the case for multi‑agent designs in policy evaluation.

## LLM Agents for Economic and Social Simulation

Closest to our setting are LLM‑agent simulations of economic and societal phenomena. EconAgent [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.10436) uses LLM‑empowered heterogeneous agents to reproduce classic macroeconomic regularities—Phillips, Okun, and Beveridge relationships—and outperforms rule‑based and RL‑based baselines on the Foundation environment used by prior AI‑Economist work, demonstrating that LLM agents can plausibly model realistic labor and consumption decisions. AucArena [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.05746) evaluates LLM agents' strategic planning and budget management in auction markets, while CompeteAI [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.17512) studies competition dynamics between restaurant and customer agents in a virtual town, yielding micro‑ and macro‑level findings consistent with market theory. Beyond markets, S³ [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.14984) simulates emotion, attitude, and information propagation on real social networks; WarAgent [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.17227) reconstructs historical conflicts to identify triggers of war; and Agent Hospital [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.02957) evolves medical LLM agents through simulated clinical encounters. At larger scale, AgentSociety [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.08691) integrates over ten thousand LLM‑driven agents with a realistic urban environment to study policy interventions such as universal basic income and external shocks, illustrating how generative agents can serve as a testbed for redistributive policies.

## Positioning

Our work sits at the intersection of these lines: like the AI Economist [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.13332), [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.02755) and TaxAI [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.16307), we design adaptive tax rates in a heterogeneous‑agent macroeconomic simulator, but instead of RL policies we drive both households and the government with LLMs following the generative‑agent paradigm [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442), [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.11970). By coupling LLM‑based taxpayer heterogeneity [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.10436) with an LLM TaxAgent that iteratively updates tax rates, we obtain a scalable, data‑driven fiscal‑policy laboratory that improves on the Saez benchmark and U.S. federal income tax baselines while accommodating irrational, bounded, and diverse behaviors that prior RL and analytical approaches struggle to capture.
