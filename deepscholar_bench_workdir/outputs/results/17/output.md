# Related Work

## Standard Semi-Supervised Learning

Modern semi-supervised learning (SSL) is dominated by two complementary ideas: consistency regularization and pseudo-labeling. Temporal Ensembling [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.02242) and Mean Teacher [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.01780) enforce agreement between predictions of the same input under different perturbations or between a student and an exponentially-averaged teacher. Unsupervised Data Augmentation [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.12848) further shows that strong data augmentation can be used as the perturbation source for consistency training. Holistic frameworks such as MixMatch [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.02249) and ReMixMatch [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.09785) combine sharpening, MixUp, and distribution alignment, while FixMatch [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.07685) merges high-confidence pseudo-labels of weakly-augmented views with consistency on strongly-augmented views, becoming the de facto backbone for subsequent SSL research. Extensions such as FlexMatch [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.08263), FreeMatch [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.07246), and SoftMatch [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.10921) refine the fixed confidence threshold via curriculum, self-adaptive, or Gaussian-weighted schemes to balance pseudo-label quantity and quality. Similarity-driven variants like CoMatch [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.11183), SimMatch [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.06915), and SimMatchV2 [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.06692) additionally exploit graph or instance-level consistency, and self-supervised pretraining [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.10029) provides strong representations that boost SSL. All of these methods, however, presuppose that unlabeled data comes from the same class distribution as the labeled set, an assumption that is routinely violated in practice.

## Open-Set Semi-Supervised Learning

When the unlabeled set contains samples from unknown categories, naive SSL propagates biased pseudo-labels through outliers and can perform worse than purely supervised training. Multi-Task Curriculum Framework [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.11330) was one of the first methods to jointly estimate an OOD score and train the classifier, whereas T2T [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.05617) recycles unlabeled OOD samples via cross-modal matching. OpenMatch [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.14148) combines FixMatch with one-vs-all classifiers and open-set soft-consistency regularization to explicitly reject outliers. IOMatch [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.13168) unifies inlier classification and outlier detection through a joint label space, and SSB [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.10572) shows that carefully designed simple baselines are competitive with more elaborate open-set SSL pipelines. Weight-Aware Distillation [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.11874) instead down-weights unreliable unlabeled samples through distillation, while Out-of-Distributed Semantic Pruning [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18158) removes semantic components that are shared between inliers and outliers to obtain a purer representation of the closed set. All of these approaches ultimately rely on discrepancy signals derived from a single model trained mostly on the (small) labeled set, and therefore degrade sharply when labeled data is scarce — the exact regime motivating our work.

## Diversity, Disagreement, and Model Ensembles for Robustness

The idea of using disagreement between diverse hypotheses to identify ambiguous or out-of-distribution data has a long history. Recent work on learning from underspecified data by Lee et al. [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.03418) trains a diverse collection of predictors that agree on labeled data but disagree on unlabeled or OOD points; the disagreement can then be used for OOD detection or human-in-the-loop disambiguation. Similar ideas have been extended to curriculum reinforcement learning, where out-of-distribution disagreement between ensemble members is used as an intrinsic goal signal [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.19261). Our Diversify and Conquer (DAC) framework follows this line of thinking but adapts it to open-set SSL: instead of paying the cost of training multiple independent networks, we construct a collection of differently-biased heads through a single training run that share a common backbone. By explicitly encouraging heads to be differently biased toward outliers while remaining consistent on inliers, we obtain a disagreement measure that remains reliable even when the labeled data is severely underspecified.

## Position of Our Work

Our method connects the two lines above. Compared with existing open-set SSL methods [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.11330), [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.14148), [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.13168), [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.10572), [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.11874), [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18158) that use single-model prediction discrepancies, DAC exploits multi-head disagreement induced by different biases toward the unlabeled distribution. Compared with prior diversity-based OOD approaches [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.03418), [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.19261), DAC integrates diversification directly into a modern SSL pipeline built on FixMatch-style consistency [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.07685) and confidence-based pseudo-labeling [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.08263), [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.07246), enabling robust outlier detection and improved inlier classification in the low-label, high-outlier regime that has proved most challenging for prior work.
