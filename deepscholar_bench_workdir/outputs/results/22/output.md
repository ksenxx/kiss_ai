# Related Work

**Unsupervised Domain Adaptation for Semantic Segmentation (UDA-SS).** Early UDA-SS methods bridged the source–target gap through adversarial alignment at the pixel, feature, or output level. FCNs in the Wild [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.02649) was one of the first works to adapt fully convolutional networks across domains via global and local alignment, while CyCADA [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.03213) combined cycle-consistent image translation with feature-level adaptation. AdaptSegNet [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.10349) showed that aligning the structured output space is more effective than aligning intermediate features, and ADVENT [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.12833) additionally minimized target-domain entropy for confident predictions. BDL [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.10620) unified image translation and segmentation adaptation in a bidirectional learning framework. More recently, self-training and consistency-based pipelines have dominated the field: DACS [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.08702) and ClassMix [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.07936) mix source and target content to produce reliable pseudo-labels, while DAFormer [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.14887), HRDA [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.13132), and MIC [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.01322) integrate Transformer backbones such as SegFormer [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.15203) and SETR [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.15840) with multi-resolution and masked-image consistency training, achieving state-of-the-art results on GTA5→Cityscapes and Synthia→Cityscapes. Weakly supervised variants such as [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.15176) further leverage image-level tags in the target domain.

**Prototype- and contrast-based adaptation.** A parallel line of work exploits class prototypes and contrastive objectives to shape a domain-invariant feature space. ProDA [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.10979) denoises pseudo-labels via prototypical distances and progressively learns the target structure. Category Contrast [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.02885) introduces a category-aware contrastive loss for visual UDA, and ProCA [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.06654) enforces prototypical contrast between intra-class and inter-class features across domains. SePiCo [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.08808) proposes semantic-guided pixel contrast that models each class as a distribution rather than a single centroid, and Contrastive Learning for Label-Efficient Semantic Segmentation [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.06985) demonstrates the utility of pixel-level contrast in low-label regimes. Domain generalization approaches such as SAN-SAW [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.00822) similarly use semantic-aware whitening/normalization to encourage class-discriminative but domain-invariant representations. Our Domain-Specific Prototype-based Distinction (DSPD) module differs from these methods by explicitly maintaining *two* prototypes per class—one per domain—so that common-class ambiguity with private classes can be resolved by finer-grained matching.

**Category-shift domain adaptation.** Classical UDA assumes identical label sets, which rarely holds in practice. Partial DA [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.04205) considers a target label space that is a subset of the source, whereas Open-Set DA by Backpropagation [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.10427) tackles unknown target-only classes. Universal Domain Adaptation (UniDA) removes any assumption on category overlap: DANCE [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.07953) unifies neighborhood clustering with entropy separation to jointly recognize common classes and reject private ones; OVANet [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.03344) learns one-vs-all inter-class boundaries to threshold unknowns; and Divergence Optimization [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.00246) addresses noisy universal transfer. UniOT [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.17067) formulates UniDA via optimal transport, while more recent methods such as CAM [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.11862) and the source-free GLC [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07110) further generalize UniDA to attention-based matching and model reuse settings. Related source-free approaches such as NRC [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.04202) exploit intrinsic neighborhood structure to cope with category shifts without source data. Open compound settings [29](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.03403) also handle mixtures of unseen target domains.

**Open-set and universal adaptation for segmentation.** Extending category-shift adaptation from classification to dense prediction is still nascent. Open-world semantic segmentation for LiDAR point clouds [30](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.01452) rejects unknown categories via redundancy classifiers, and multi-modal prototype methods [31](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.02003) explore open-set segmentation in 2D. Closest to our setting, BUS [32](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.19899) formulates Open-Set Domain Adaptation for Semantic Segmentation (OSDA-SS) with boundary- and unknown-shape-aware objectives, but still assumes prior knowledge of category configurations. In contrast, our UniDA-SS setting removes any assumption about which classes are common or private, and our UniMAP framework combines domain-specific prototypes (DSPD) with Target-based Image Matching (TIM) to explicitly boost common-class confidence in the target domain.
