# Related Works

**Fashion Compatibility Learning.** Early approaches to outfit recommendation focused on learning compatibility relationships among fashion items. [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.05691) modeled outfits as sequences and trained a bidirectional LSTM to sequentially predict items while jointly learning a visual-semantic embedding space. [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.09196) introduced the Polyvore Outfits dataset and proposed type-aware embeddings that capture both item similarity within categories and cross-type compatibility, achieving improvements on outfit compatibility prediction and fill-in-the-blank tasks. [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1902.08009) represented outfits as subgraphs of a Fashion Graph and proposed Node-wise Graph Neural Networks to model higher-order item interactions through attention mechanisms. More recent work by [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.04812) applied Transformers with task-specific tokens and set-wise ranking loss to learn outfit-level representations, demonstrating superior performance on compatibility prediction and complementary item retrieval. [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.01748) extended compatibility modeling to scene-based recommendation, proposing the "Complete the Look" task that recommends products compatible with real-world scene images using CNNs and attention mechanisms.

**Generative Models for Fashion.** Recent advances in generative AI have enabled the synthesis of fashion items for outfit generation. [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.17279) introduced the Generative Outfit Recommendation task and proposed DiFashion, a diffusion-based model that generates multiple fashion images in parallel conditioned on category, compatibility, and personalization constraints using Classifier-Free Guidance. This work demonstrated the potential of diffusion models to generate complete personalized outfits on the iFashion and Polyvore-U datasets. However, these generative approaches often lack diversity and rely on supervised learning paradigms without explicit alignment to human preferences.

**Direct Preference Optimization and Human Feedback.** The alignment of generative models to human preferences has been extensively studied in language and vision domains. [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18290) introduced Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), a simpler alternative to reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) that directly optimizes a policy satisfying human preferences under a classification objective, eliminating the need for a separate reward model. [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12908) adapted DPO to text-to-image diffusion models by reformulating the objective using the evidence lower bound, achieving significant improvements over Stable Diffusion XL on visual appeal and prompt alignment using 851K pairwise preferences. Alternative approaches include [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13301), which cast denoising as a multi-step decision-making problem and applied policy gradient RL to optimize diffusion models for objectives like aesthetic quality and prompt alignment, and [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.05977), which trained ImageReward as a general-purpose human preference reward model on 137K expert comparisons for evaluating and fine-tuning text-to-image models. These methods have shown promise in aligning generative models with human preferences but have not been applied to the fashion domain, where personalization and compatibility constraints present unique challenges.
