# Related Works

## Dense retrieval as the standard neural baseline

Before generative retrieval emerged, dense retrieval had become the dominant neural paradigm in information retrieval. Dense Passage Retrieval [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.04906) learns dual-encoder representations for questions and passages and retrieves via approximate nearest-neighbor search over pre-computed embeddings. ColBERT [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.12832) refines this approach with a late-interaction mechanism that preserves token-level matching, while ANCE [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00808) improves training by mining hard negatives from an asynchronously updated approximate nearest-neighbor index. These dense retrievers still serve as the strongest non-generative baselines against which generative retrieval (GR) methods, including our study over dynamic corpora, are compared.

## Foundations of generative retrieval

The generative retrieval paradigm was popularized by GENRE [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.00904), which autoregressively generates entity names as identifiers using constrained beam search, and by the Differentiable Search Index (DSI) [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.06991), which trains a single transformer to map queries directly to document identifiers (docids). DynamicRetriever [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.00537) similarly stores the corpus in model parameters and generates docids at inference time. These works established GR as a promising alternative to sparse and dense retrieval, but they all evaluate on static corpora and largely overlook the dynamics of real-world search engines that we investigate.

## Numeric-based docid designs

A prominent line of GR research assigns each document a compact numeric identifier and trains the model to generate it. NCI [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.02743) uses a hierarchical k-means tree over document embeddings to produce semantically structured integer docids, and introduces a prefix-aware decoder. GenRet [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.04171) learns discrete document codes end-to-end through a differentiable tokenizer, while RIPOR [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09134) constructs relevance-aware quantized codes and introduces prefix-oriented ranking optimization to scale GR to MS MARCO-sized collections. Ultron [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.09257) further combines URL-derived and clustered numeric ids in a model-based indexer. Our reproduction over evolving corpora shows that, despite their efficiency, all such numeric-based docids struggle to generalize to newly added documents — a limitation we trace back to overfitting on the initial training corpus.

## Text-based and multi-view docid designs

Another family of GR methods uses natural-language strings as docids. SEAL [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.10628) autoregressively generates arbitrary substrings of documents grounded via an FM-index. MINDER [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16675) enhances GR with multi-view identifiers such as titles, substrings, and pseudo-queries. TSGen [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13859) represents documents as unordered term sets and decodes them permutation-invariantly, and LLM-URL [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.09225) shows that large language models can generate URLs as docids in a zero-shot fashion. Our analysis complements these works by identifying three properties of text-based docids — semantic alignment with pretrained knowledge, fine-grained design, and high lexical diversity — that are decisive for generalization to unseen documents.

## Scaling, training, and ranking-oriented GR

Several works address indexing–retrieval mismatches and scaling. DSI-QG [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.10128) augments indexing with generated pseudo-queries so that documents are indexed under query-like text. SE-DSI [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.15115) leverages elaborative descriptions and rehearsal contents inspired by human learning strategies. Pradeep et al. [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.11841) systematically study how GR scales to millions of passages, revealing the difficulty of matching dense retrievers at scale. LTRGR [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.15222) casts GR as a ranking problem and directly optimizes passage-level ranking objectives, PAG [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.14600) plans docid generation via simultaneous set- and sequence-based decoding, and DGR [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.10769) distills knowledge from cross-encoder rerankers into the generative retriever. These techniques are orthogonal to our focus on dynamic corpora but share our interest in making GR practically deployable.

## Generative retrieval over dynamic and continual corpora

Most directly related to our work are GR methods designed for evolving document collections. CorpusBrain [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.07652) pre-trains a generative retrieval backbone for knowledge-intensive tasks, and its extension CorpusBrain++ [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.16767) proposes a continual generative pre-training framework together with the KILT++ benchmark for continually arriving documents. CLEVER [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.14968) tackles the same continual setting with incremental product quantization and memory-augmented learning to encode new documents without forgetting. Whereas these approaches propose new training procedures for continual GR, we instead systematically reproduce and dissect representative GR methods over dynamic corpora, analyze *why* text-based docids generalize better than numeric ones, and, informed by these insights, design a training-free multi-docid scheme that combines the efficiency of numeric docids with the effectiveness of text-based docids.
