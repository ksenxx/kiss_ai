# Related Work

## Sequential Recommendation with Recurrent and Attentive Architectures

Deep sequential recommendation has evolved rapidly from recurrent models to attention-based architectures. GRU4Rec [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1511.06939) pioneered the use of gated recurrent units for session-based recommendation, encoding user histories left-to-right into hidden states. With the rise of the Transformer [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762), self-attention was quickly adapted to next-item prediction: SASRec [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.09781) demonstrated that a causal self-attentive encoder can outperform RNN and CNN baselines while remaining efficient, and BERT4Rec [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.06690) further improved representation quality by adopting a bidirectional masked language modeling ("Cloze") objective. More recently, Transformer-based approaches have been pushed to industrial scale by RecFormer [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13731), which treats items as textual descriptions to learn language-model-style sequence representations, and by HSTU [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.17152), which reformulates recommendation as generative sequential transduction with trillion-parameter transducers. Despite their strong performance, these attention-based models suffer from quadratic complexity in the sequence length and often struggle with users whose behavior spans highly diverse item categories.

## Attention Alternatives and Frequency-Domain Models

To mitigate the shortcomings of vanilla self-attention, several works redesign the token-mixing layer for recommendation. FMLP-Rec [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.13556) shows that an all-MLP filter operating in the frequency domain can match or surpass Transformer baselines while denoising sparse behavior signals. FEARec [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.09184) hybridizes time- and frequency-domain attention with a frequency-based contrastive objective to capture both low- and high-frequency user preferences. LRURec [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.02367) revisits recurrence through linear recurrent units, providing parallel training and linear-time inference. Our preference-aware attention builds on this line of work, but explicitly disentangles similar-item and diverse-preference signals rather than uniformly mixing tokens.

## State-Space Models for Sequential Recommendation

The recent success of structured state-space models (S4 [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.00396)) and the selective state-space Mamba architecture [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.00752) has motivated a wave of SSM-based sequential recommenders that process variable-length histories in linear time. Mamba4Rec [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.03900) was the first to import selective SSMs into sequential recommendation, demonstrating a favorable effectiveness–efficiency tradeoff over RNN and attention baselines. RecMamba [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.16371) further exploits Mamba's selective mechanism for lifelong sequential recommendation with very long histories, and SIGMA [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.11451) introduces selective gating on top of Mamba to strengthen partial-order modeling for user behavior. A broader survey of SSMs as Transformer alternatives is given in Mamba-360 [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16112). While these methods excel at compressing temporal dynamics, they do not natively model similarity- and diversity-aware item relations, which motivates our synergistic combination of SSM and preference-aware attention.

## Self-Supervised and Contrastive Sequential Recommenders

A complementary line of work injects self-supervision to alleviate sparsity. CL4SRec [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.14395) augments interaction sequences (crop/mask/reorder) under a contrastive objective, while DuoRec [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.05730) directly addresses representation degeneration by contrasting semantically equivalent sequences. MAERec [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.04619) leverages a graph-based masked autoencoder to distill global item-transition signals into sequential encoders. STAR-Rec is orthogonal to these objectives and can be combined with them.

## Mixture-of-Experts for Adaptive Modeling

Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures enable models to route inputs to specialized sub-modules. The Sparsely-Gated MoE layer [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1701.06538) established conditional computation over thousands of experts, and Switch Transformer [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.03961) scaled this idea to trillion-parameter language models with a simplified top-1 routing scheme. Inspired by these designs, STAR-Rec introduces a *sequence-level* MoE that adaptively dispatches heterogeneous behavioral patterns—focused category-specific browsing versus wide-ranging exploration—to experts that specialize in preference-aware attention and state-space modeling, respectively, unifying the strengths of both families under a single architecture.
