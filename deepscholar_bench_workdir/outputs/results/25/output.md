# Related Work

## Claim Verification over Unstructured Evidence

Automated claim verification has traditionally relied on unstructured textual corpora as the primary source of evidence. The FEVER benchmark [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.05355) established the canonical retrieve–then–verify pipeline over Wikipedia, and subsequent datasets extended this paradigm to multi-hop reasoning (HoVer [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.03088)) and to mixed evidence combining tables and sentences (FEVEROUS [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.05707)). Early graph-inspired verifiers such as GEAR [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.01843) and the Kernel Graph Attention Network (KGAT) [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.09796) impose an evidence graph over retrieved sentences to aggregate signals across multiple documents, while MultiVerS [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.01640) demonstrates full-document context modeling for scientific claims. A comprehensive survey by Guo et al. [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.11896) situates these systems within the broader automated fact-checking landscape and highlights their reliance on unstructured text, which limits their capacity to exploit semantically rich structured knowledge.

## LLM-based Fact-Checking Pipelines

Recent work leverages large language models as reasoning engines for verification. ProgramFC [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.12744) decomposes complex claims into executable sub-programs whose steps invoke retrieval and question-answering modules. HiSS [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.00305) prompts an LLM to split a claim into sub-claims and verify each via hierarchical question answering, and FOLK [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.05253) grounds LLM verdicts using first-order-logic-guided reasoning traces. FactLLaMA [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00240) instruction-tunes an LLM with external retrieved evidence, and ClaimDecomp [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.06938) generates literal and implied sub-questions to expose the hidden claims that must be checked. These pipelines inherit the general prompting techniques of chain-of-thought reasoning [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.11903) and interleaved reasoning-and-acting [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629), yet, similar to earlier systems, they operate primarily over unstructured text and provide no principled interface to structured knowledge bases.

## Knowledge-Graph-Augmented Verification and Reasoning

Knowledge graphs offer explicit relational structure that complements textual evidence. FactKG [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.06590) introduces a benchmark that specifically requires reasoning over DBpedia triples, and KG-GPT [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.11220) proposes a three-stage sentence-to-triple framework for KG-based claim verification and question answering. Think-on-Graph [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.07697) has an LLM interactively explore a KG through beam search over relations, while Reasoning-on-Graphs (RoG) [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01061) plans faithful relation paths whose grounding on the KG is used to produce interpretable answers. StructGPT [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.09645) equips an LLM with tool-like interfaces to query tables, databases, and KGs, and Chain-of-Knowledge [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13269) dynamically adapts intermediate reasoning to heterogeneous knowledge sources. Earlier neuro-symbolic models such as QA-GNN [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.06378) tightly couple a language model with a graph neural network over retrieved subgraphs, and KG-augmented dialogue models [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18846) inject subgraph context into generation. A recent survey by Pan et al. [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.08302) outlines the emerging roadmap for unifying LLMs and KGs, motivating architectures that use LLMs both to interpret claims and to guide KG traversal.

## Retrieval Augmentation and Graph-Aware Retrieval

Our approach is also related to retrieval-augmented generation. REALM [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.08909) and RAG [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401) established the joint training of a retriever and a generator for knowledge-intensive tasks, and GraphRAG [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16130) extends this idea by constructing entity–relation graphs and performing community-aware retrieval for query-focused summarization.

## Positioning of ClaimPKG

In contrast to unstructured-text verifiers [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.05355), [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.03088), [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.05707), [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.01843), [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.09796), and to LLM-only fact-checking pipelines [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.12744), [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.00305), [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.05253), [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00240), ClaimPKG directly targets structured KG evidence. Unlike prior KG-reasoning systems that either rely on a single monolithic LLM to walk the graph [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.07697), [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01061), [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.09645) or use fixed multi-stage prompting [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.11220), ClaimPKG adapts a lightweight specialized LLM to translate the claim into pseudo-subgraphs that guide a dedicated subgraph retriever, before delegating verdict generation and justification to a general-purpose LLM. This design realizes the LLM–KG unification advocated by [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.08302) while inheriting the interpretability of path-based reasoning [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01061) and the flexibility of retrieval-augmented generation [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401), and it further generalizes zero-shot to unstructured benchmarks such as HoVer [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.03088) and FEVEROUS [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.05707).
