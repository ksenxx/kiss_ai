# Related Works

## Brain Tumor Segmentation with U-Net Variants

Automatic brain tumor segmentation on multi-modal MRI has been a central focus of the medical imaging community, driven in large part by the annual BraTS challenge and its curated benchmarks [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.02629). The U-Net architecture [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1505.04597) established an encoder–decoder design with skip connections that has become the de-facto backbone for medical image segmentation. Its volumetric extensions, 3D U-Net [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.06650) and V-Net [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.04797), further adapted the paradigm to volumetric MRI/CT data by replacing 2D operators with fully 3D convolutions and by introducing objectives such as the Dice loss. Building on these foundations, Kamnitsas et al. proposed DeepMedic, a dual-pathway multi-scale 3D CNN combined with a fully connected CRF for accurate brain lesion segmentation [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1603.05959), and subsequently the EMMA ensemble that won the BraTS 2017 challenge by combining heterogeneous architectures [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.01468). Attention mechanisms have also been integrated into U-Net-style networks to focus computation on informative spatial regions [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.03999).

## nnU-Net and Its Extensions

The nnU-Net framework [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.10486) reframed segmentation performance as an outcome of careful, data-driven configuration of otherwise vanilla U-Nets and has since been consolidated into a self-configuring pipeline that generalizes across many biomedical tasks [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08128). Isensee et al. previously demonstrated the strength of well-trained U-Nets on BraTS 2017 [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.10508) and BraTS 2018 with the "No New-Net" contribution [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.10483). More recent BraTS winners continue to build on nnU-Net, for example by adopting larger networks, group normalization, and axial attention in the decoder [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.04653). Despite its strong accuracy, a well-known limitation of nnU-Net is the heavy training budget (typically 1000 epochs per fold with 5-fold cross-validation and ensembling), which motivates the fast-training and pre-trained-initialization strategies pursued in this work.

## Transformer-Based Volumetric Segmentation

Transformers have recently been integrated into 3D medical segmentation to capture long-range context. TransBTS combines a 3D CNN with a Transformer bottleneck for multi-modal brain tumor segmentation [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.04430), while UNETR employs a pure Transformer encoder attached to a convolutional decoder for volumetric segmentation [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.10504). Swin UNETR extends this design using hierarchical Swin Transformers and reports strong performance on brain tumor segmentation [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.01266). nnFormer further interleaves convolutional and self-attention blocks and is systematically benchmarked against nnU-Net on volumetric tasks [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2109.03201). Although these models advance the state of the art, most are trained from scratch and inherit the same expensive training regime we aim to alleviate.

## Transfer Learning and 2D-to-3D Weight Reuse

Because large-scale 3D annotated datasets remain scarce, transferring 2D representations pre-trained on ImageNet – such as those learned by VGG [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/1409.1556) and ResNet [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1512.03385) – has been explored extensively. Ghafoorian et al. investigated cross-domain transfer learning for brain lesion segmentation [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/1702.07841), while Med3D pre-trains generic 3D encoders on aggregated medical datasets [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.00625) and Models Genesis learns self-supervised 3D representations from unlabeled CT/MRI volumes [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.06912). Closer to our approach, "Reinventing 2D Convolutions for 3D Images" introduces Axial-Coronal-Sagittal (ACS) convolutions that factorize a 3D kernel into three orthogonal 2D views, enabling direct reuse of 2D pre-trained weights in 3D pipelines [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.10477). Similar factorizations have proven effective in video understanding: I3D inflates 2D filters into 3D by replicating them along the temporal axis [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/1705.07750), R(2+1)D decomposes spatiotemporal convolutions into successive 2D and 1D operations [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.11248), and S3D combines separable 3D convolutions with careful architectural placement of 2D and 3D blocks [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/1712.04851). Our two strategies for lifting 2D ImageNet weights into a 3D nnU-Net encoder build directly on this line of work, aiming to preserve the learned filter relationships critical for effective information propagation in the volumetric setting.

## Joint Classification and Segmentation

Finally, coupling auxiliary classification objectives with segmentation has been shown to enrich learned representations. In the brain tumor setting, glioma grading is a natural proxy task whose encoder can be transferred to the segmentation network, providing complementary semantic cues that particularly help challenging sub-region labels such as the enhancing tumor and tumor core defined in the BraTS benchmark [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.02629). Our joint classification-and-segmentation model follows this multi-task philosophy while remaining compatible with the fast-training nnU-Net configurations discussed above [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08128).
