# Related Work

## Neural Text-to-Speech Synthesis

Modern TTS has evolved from encoder–decoder attention models such as Tacotron [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.10135) and its Transformer variant [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.08895), toward non-autoregressive architectures like FastSpeech [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.09263) and FastSpeech 2 [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.04558), and fully end-to-end systems such as VITS [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.06103) and NaturalSpeech [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.04421). More recently, neural-codec language-model approaches — AudioLM [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.03143), VALL-E [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.02111), and SPEAR-TTS [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.03540) — have achieved highly natural, zero-shot speech generation by treating speech as discrete token sequences. While these systems dramatically improve naturalness, they expose only limited external knobs for prosody or pronunciation, motivating post-hoc control techniques such as ours that operate directly on the internal representations of an already-trained model.

## Prosody Modeling and Control

A long line of work has augmented TTS with dedicated prosody modules. Global Style Tokens [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.09017) learn an unsupervised inventory of style embeddings that can be mixed at inference, and reference-encoder–based prosody transfer [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.09047) conditions synthesis on a target utterance to copy its prosodic contour. FastSpeech 2 [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.04558) explicitly predicts pitch, energy, and duration so that these variables can be manually overridden, and VITS [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.06103) exposes a stochastic duration predictor for rhythm variation. All of these require training-time architectural commitments: adding a style encoder, prosody predictors, or specific conditioning inputs. In contrast, we seek a mechanism that grants comparable prosodic control over any pretrained model, without retraining or auxiliary modules.

## Pronunciation Modeling and Mispronunciation Correction

Traditional TTS pipelines rely on grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) front ends [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.06338), [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.10716), typically backed by curated lexicons and phoneme dictionaries. Dict-TTS [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.02147) integrates online dictionaries to disambiguate polyphones, while LRSpeech [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2008.03687) shows how pronunciation lexicons can be leveraged in extremely low-resource settings. To reduce lexicon dependence, data-driven G2P representations have been proposed for lexicon-free TTS [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.10465). In the assessment literature, mispronunciation detection and diagnosis has been tackled via acoustic-phonetic-linguistic embeddings [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.07274), text-aware end-to-end models [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.07289), and multilingual L1-aware frameworks [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.07719). These approaches presuppose access to reliable phoneme inventories or L2 corpora — a strong assumption we relax by editing model activations to correct specific mispronunciations without touching the front end.

## Activation Editing and Representation Engineering

Our method is inspired by recent work that manipulates hidden states of pretrained models. Causal-mediation analysis [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.12265) and causal abstractions of neural networks [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.02997) established interchange interventions on internal activations as a tool for interpretability. ROME [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.05262) leveraged such interventions to locate and rewrite factual associations directly in the weights of GPT-style models. Building on this, inference-time steering methods — Inference-Time Intervention [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.03341), activation-space steering of language models [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.10248), and Representation Engineering [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01405) — demonstrate that adding low-dimensional direction vectors to hidden states can control high-level attributes such as truthfulness or style without any gradient updates. To date, these techniques have almost exclusively been studied in the language domain; we transfer this perspective to speech synthesis and show that carefully constructed counterfactual activation edits can serve as controllers for prosody and pronunciation.

## Counterfactual Explanations

Finally, our formulation is influenced by the broader literature on counterfactual reasoning for opaque models. Wachter et al. introduced counterfactual explanations for automated decisions [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.00399), Goyal et al. extended the idea to visual classifiers with counterfactual visual explanations [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.07451), and DiCE explored diverse counterfactuals for tabular predictors [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.07697). We adapt this counterfactual lens to a generative speech model: rather than asking what input change would flip a decision, we ask what minimal activation change would produce a targeted prosodic or phonetic outcome, and use the resulting edit as a controllable, model-agnostic intervention.
