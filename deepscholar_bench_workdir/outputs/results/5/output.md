# Related Work

## General-purpose Text Embeddings

Learning universal text representations has been a long-standing research goal. Sentence-BERT [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.10084) adapts pretrained BERT with a Siamese network so that sentence-level similarity can be computed efficiently through cosine distance in a shared embedding space. Contrastive objectives were later shown to substantially improve representation quality: SimCSE [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08821) demonstrated that simple dropout-based positive pairs, together with in-batch negatives, yield strong sentence embeddings, while Sentence-T5 [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.08877) scaled encoder-decoder backbones to produce robust generic sentence encoders. For open-domain retrieval, Dense Passage Retrieval [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.04906) established the dual-encoder paradigm using hard-negative mining, and Contriever [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.09118) extended this direction with fully unsupervised contrastive pretraining. Subsequent work scaled these ideas: GTR [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.07899) showed that very large dual encoders generalize well across retrieval domains, OpenAI's cpt-text embeddings [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.10005) coupled contrastive pretraining with massive weak supervision, and E5 [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.03533) as well as BGE (C-Pack) [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.07597) further combined web-scale weak supervision with high-quality fine-tuning to produce widely used general-purpose encoders. The MTEB benchmark [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.07316) has become the standard evaluation suite for such encoders, revealing however that a single static embedding often cannot capture the many different notions of similarity users may require. Our work follows this line of research but explicitly targets the case in which one representation must be re-shaped on-the-fly to match a user-specified notion of relevance, rather than being learned once and frozen.

## Instruction-following and Task-aware Embeddings

To make embeddings responsive to user intent, INSTRUCTOR [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.09741) proposed prepending natural-language task instructions to each input and training a single encoder over 330 diverse tasks, showing that instructions can steer a shared embedding space toward different downstream objectives. TART [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.09260) extends this idea to retrieval by training a task-aware retriever on a large collection of retrieval datasets paired with human-written instructions, while Promptagator [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11755) uses a few natural-language prompts to synthesize task-specific queries for training specialized retrievers. More recent LLM-based encoders further blur the line between generation and representation: E5-Mistral [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.00368) fine-tunes a decoder-only LLM on synthetic instruction-conditioned data, GRIT [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09906) unifies generative and representational instruction tuning inside one model, and NV-Embed [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17428) introduces architectural and training refinements that push instruction-tuned LLM embedders to state-of-the-art results on MTEB. Promptriever [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.11136) shows that retrievers can be prompted like language models when trained on instance-level instructions. A different line of work reframes instruction following at inference time: InBedder [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09642) treats each instruction as a question and encodes the model-generated answer as the embedding, obtaining highly instruction-adaptive representations at the cost of per-instruction LLM decoding over the corpus. Dedicated benchmarks such as FollowIR [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.15246) and INSTRUCTIR [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.14334) have shown that many "instruction-aware" retrievers largely react to keywords in the instructions and struggle with genuinely novel user directives, motivating both better training data and more flexible inference-time adaptation.

## Positioning of GSTransform

All of the above instruction-aware approaches share a fundamental cost: whenever the instruction changes, the corpus must be re-encoded by a heavy encoder [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.09741)[12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.09260)[14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.00368)[15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09906)[16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17428)[18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09642), which is prohibitive on large-scale collections. GSTransform is complementary to these efforts: instead of learning a new encoder or re-encoding the corpus, it operates on top of the strong generic embeddings produced by models such as E5 [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.03533), BGE [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.07597), and their LLM-based successors [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.00368)[16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.17428), and learns a lightweight guided space transformation that realigns pre-computed vectors with the user instruction in real time, delivering large speedups while retaining the instruction-awareness targeted by prior work.
