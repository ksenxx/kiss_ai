# Related Work

## Single-Domain Recommendation

Traditional recommenders learn a single model tailored to a single business scenario. Factorization-based deep models such as DeepFM [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.04247) combine low- and high-order feature interactions for click-through rate (CTR) prediction, while user-behavior models like Deep Interest Network (DIN) [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.06978) adaptively activate historical interests with respect to the candidate item. Sequential recommenders such as SASRec [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1808.09781) further exploit self-attention over user histories. Although these architectures achieve strong accuracy within a domain, they neither share knowledge with related domains nor address the distribution shift that arises when serving heterogeneous scenarios, motivating multi-domain extensions.

## Multi-Domain Recommendation

Multi-Domain Recommendation (MDR) trains one model to jointly serve multiple business domains by disentangling domain-shared from domain-specific signals. STAR [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.11427) pioneers this line with a star-topology in which a shared centered network is element-wise combined with per-domain sub-networks, together with partitioned normalization. Subsequent works refine the shared/specific decomposition through hierarchical or gated structures: HiNet [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.06095) organizes scenario and task information into a hierarchical extraction module, SAR-Net [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.06475) introduces scenario-aware attention over cross-scenario behaviors, and PEPNet [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.01115) infuses personalized prior information through embedding and parameter personalization for multi-domain multi-task ranking. ADI [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.09672) learns adaptive domain interest representations that route information across domains, while AdaSparse [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.13108) learns adaptively sparse sub-networks per domain to reduce interference. Meta-learning based schemes such as M2M [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.06814) and the model-agnostic MAMDR framework [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.12524) treat each domain as a task, generating domain-specific parameters from a shared meta-learner. HAMUR [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.06217) further plugs a domain-conditioned hyper-adapter into arbitrary backbones. Despite their empirical success, these approaches share a common assumption: the transferable knowledge is uniformly beneficial across every domain, and the transfer structure is fixed once trained. When domains conflict or are of uneven quality, indiscriminate sharing induces the Negative Transfer Problem (NTP) that our work aims to alleviate.

## Cross-Domain Recommendation and Negative Transfer

Cross-Domain Recommendation (CDR) is closely related but generally focuses on transferring from a source to a target domain to overcome cold start or data sparsity, as summarized in the CDR survey [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.01696). CoNet [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.06769) transfers knowledge through cross-connected feed-forward networks, and DDTCDR [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.05189) enforces bi-directional dual transfer between paired domains. Beyond pairwise setups, MetaHeac [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.14688) uses meta hybrid experts to expand audiences across advertising scenarios, and continual transfer learning has been deployed on Taobao to sequentially propagate CTR knowledge across domains [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.05728). Most of these methods presume that the source domain is a priori helpful and rely on hand-picked domain pairs; they do not explicitly quantify how similar two domains are, nor do they dynamically decide, at training time, which domains should be transferred from. Consequently, negative transfer remains a well-documented risk whenever the source distribution diverges from the target.

## Prototype-Based Representation Learning

Our similar-domain selection principle draws on prototype learning, popularized by Prototypical Networks for few-shot classification [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1703.05175), where each class is summarized by the mean of its embeddings and queries are classified by distance to prototypes. We instead learn a prototype per domain and use prototype distances, together with supervised domain-metric signals, to measure inter-domain gaps and dynamically pick similar domains for each target. To our knowledge, this is the first attempt to explicitly measure domain-level distances and perform dynamic domain selection in MDR, providing a lightweight complement to the shared/specific architectures reviewed above.
