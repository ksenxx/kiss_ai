# Related Work

**Large Language Models and Their Training Demands.** The Transformer architecture [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) laid the foundation for modern large language models such as GPT-3 [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165), PaLM [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.02311), and LLaMA [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.13971). Scaling studies such as Chinchilla [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556) further motivated compute-optimal training at massive scale, driving models from billions to hundreds of billions of parameters [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.11990). These trends make efficient distributed training and accurate performance modeling indispensable for developers and system architects.

**Distributed Training Systems and Parallelism Strategies.** A large body of work has explored parallelism dimensions to fit and accelerate LLM training. Megatron-LM introduced tensor-model parallelism for Transformer blocks [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.08053), later combined with pipeline and data parallelism for GPU-cluster-scale training [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.04473). Pipeline schedulers such as GPipe [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.06965) and PipeDream [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/1806.03377) improved device utilization by overlapping micro-batches. The ZeRO family reduced optimizer, gradient, and parameter memory redundancy [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.02054), extended memory hierarchies with ZeRO-Infinity [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.07857), and compressed collectives in ZeRO++ [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.10209). Frameworks such as Colossal-AI [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.14883), Varuna [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.04007), and Merak [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.04959) unify these techniques into end-to-end training systems, while techniques such as sequence parallelism [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.13120), selective activation recomputation [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.05198), FlashAttention [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.14135), and Ring Attention [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01889) further improve throughput and memory efficiency. The vast, interacting configuration space produced by these techniques is precisely what motivates Lumos: without accurate performance estimates, choosing among them is largely empirical.

**Automatic Parallelization and Cost Models.** To navigate this configuration space, several systems build analytical or search-based cost models. FlexFlow searches the SOAP space via a simulator-guided algorithm [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1807.05358), while Alpa jointly optimizes inter- and intra-operator parallelism using a two-level compilation pipeline [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.12023). GSPMD annotates SPMD sharding at the compiler level [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.04663), Galvatron performs automatic hybrid-parallel planning for Transformers [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.13878), and AMP extends automatic strategy search to heterogeneous clusters and models [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.07297). These systems typically rely on analytical throughput and memory formulas that abstract away kernel-level and communication behaviors. Lumos complements them by providing high-fidelity, trace-driven timing estimates that can serve as ground truth or as a refined cost model within such search loops.

**Performance Modeling, Profiling, and Simulation.** Closest in spirit to our work are profiling- and trace-driven performance prediction tools. Daydream builds fine-grained dependency graphs from CUPTI traces to predict the effect of DNN optimizations [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.03318), and Habitat predicts cross-GPU DNN runtimes from a single measured trace via kernel-level scaling models [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2102.00527). DistIR proposes an intermediate representation and simulator for exploring distributed strategies [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.05426), and Chakra standardizes execution traces to enable interoperable simulation and co-design [29](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14516). Unlike these earlier tools, which target single-GPU DNN workloads or generic strategy exploration, Lumos targets modern LLM training at production scale (up to 512 H100 GPUs), captures multi-dimensional parallelism together with communication and overlap patterns, and, importantly, supports both faithful replay of observed executions and estimation of new, unmeasured configurations from previously collected traces. In doing so, Lumos bridges detailed hardware-level performance modeling with the LLM-specific system landscape that these prior efforts only partially address.
