# Related Work

## Vision Transformers

The Transformer architecture [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) was adapted to vision by the Vision Transformer (ViT), which shows that a pure self-attention model applied to sequences of image patches can match or outperform convolutional networks on large-scale image classification [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11929). Follow-up work reduces the data requirements through distillation [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.12877) and introduces hierarchical designs with shifted-window attention for general-purpose backbones [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.14030). Our work targets the efficient hardware execution of exactly this family of models, whose compute is dominated by dense matrix multiplication but whose accuracy depends on nonlinear operations such as Softmax, GELU, and LayerNorm.

## Quantization of Transformers and Vision Transformers

A large body of work has explored low-precision integer quantization for Transformer-based models. For language models, Q8BERT [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1910.06188) and I-BERT [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.01321) demonstrate 8-bit and integer-only inference, while more recent post-training methods address the accuracy cliffs of large models: LLM.int8() isolates outlier channels [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.07339), SmoothQuant migrates the quantization difficulty between activations and weights [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.10438), and GPTQ performs one-shot weight quantization via approximate second-order information [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.17323). For ViTs specifically, Liu et al. propose a similarity-aware post-training scheme [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.14156), PTQ4ViT introduces twin uniform quantization tailored to post-Softmax and post-GELU activations [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.12293), FQ-ViT quantizes LayerNorm and Softmax with power-of-two factors and log-int softmax [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.13824), Q-ViT enables fully differentiable quantization search [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.07703), and I-ViT provides integer-only inference by replacing nonlinear functions with bit-shift approximations [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.01405). These schemes highlight that plain per-tensor INT8 is insufficient for ViT nonlinearities, motivating richer numerical formats such as the one we adopt.

## Block-scaled and Microscaling Data Formats

Recent formats trade a single per-tensor scale for finer-grained shared scales. FP8 has been standardized for deep learning training and inference [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.05433). Block floating-point variants such as FlexBlock support multi-mode training accelerators [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.06673), and theoretical analyses characterize the effect of block size on quantization error [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.05470). Shared microexponent formats extend this idea with hierarchical, small exponent groups to close the accuracy gap to FP32 at very low bit widths [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.08007), and the open Microscaling (MX) specification unifies these designs into standard MXInt/MXFP formats for cross-vendor deployment [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.10537). Our accelerator builds on MXInt but is the first to co-design its arithmetic pipeline for all ViT operations, including nonlinearities, on FPGAs.

## FPGA and ASIC Accelerators for Vision Transformers

Several accelerators target ViT inference on programmable hardware. VAQF is a software-hardware co-design framework that maps low-bit ViTs to FPGAs with per-layer precision selection [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.06618). Auto-ViT-Acc explores mixed-scheme (fixed-point and power-of-two) quantization with an FPGA-aware search [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.05163). HeatViT combines hardware-efficient adaptive token pruning with 8-bit quantization [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.08110). ViTCoD prunes and polarizes the attention map to expose regular sparsity patterns for a dedicated accelerator [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.09573), and ViTALiTy unifies low-rank and sparse approximations into a linear-Taylor attention accelerator [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.05109). A key limitation shared by these works is that non-GEMM operations (Softmax, LayerNorm) are either kept at high precision or offloaded to a host CPU, introducing communication overhead. In contrast, we map every operation of the ViT onto the FPGA using MXInt datapaths.

## Hardware Optimization of Softmax and Attention

Because Softmax dominates the non-GEMM cost of Transformers, dedicated arithmetic units have been proposed. Softermax replaces the base-e exponential with a base-2 online-normalized approximation for hardware efficiency [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2103.09301). A$^3$ accelerates attention through candidate approximation and pruning [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.10941), and gradient-based learned runtime pruning skips low-magnitude Softmax outputs during inference [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.03227). Hyft designs a reconfigurable Softmax unit with a hybrid numeric format for training and inference [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.13290). Our design differs by leveraging the shared-exponent structure of MXInt to fold the exponent bias directly into the Softmax and LayerNorm pipelines, enabling large speedups over Float16 and prior FPGA-based ViT accelerators within a 1% accuracy budget.
