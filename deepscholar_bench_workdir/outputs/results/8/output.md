# Related Works

## Fairness and Bias in NLP Systems

Early studies of bias in NLP focused on distributional word representations, showing that embeddings learned from large corpora encode strong gender stereotypes and reproduce human implicit associations [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1607.06520), [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1608.07187). Subsequent work demonstrated that these biases propagate into downstream applications: contextualized embeddings such as ELMo unequally encode gender information for male and female entities and induce measurable bias in coreference systems that depend on them [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.03310). Broader critical surveys have questioned the conceptual grounding of "bias" in NLP research and called for clearer normative framings and stronger alignment between measurement and social harm [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14050), while general machine-learning surveys have catalogued the space of algorithmic fairness definitions and their trade-offs [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.09635). Classical algorithmic-fairness criteria such as Equalized Odds and Equality of Opportunity operationalize fairness as parity of accuracy-based error rates across sensitive groups [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.02413); our proposed UCerF metric is motivated by the observation that such purely accuracy-based criteria do not capture confidence disparities.

## Gender–Occupation Bias in Coreference Resolution

The most direct antecedents of our dataset are the WinoBias and Winogender benchmarks, which use Winograd-schema-style minimal pairs of sentences differing only in gendered pronouns to probe stereotype-driven coreference decisions [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.06876), [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/1804.09301). These datasets revealed that coreference systems disproportionately link gendered pronouns to occupations aligned with real-world gender statistics. Follow-up work applied causal mediation analysis to localize the components of neural language models responsible for these gender–occupation associations [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.12265). Our benchmark scales this line of inquiry, providing 31,756 gender–occupation coreference samples that address the size, diversity, and clarity limitations of prior Winograd-style resources.

## Bias Evaluation Benchmarks and Metrics for LLMs

A growing family of benchmarks measures bias in large language models along complementary axes. StereoSet [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.09456) and CrowS-Pairs [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.00133) probe stereotypical associations through masked-language-model scoring, while BBQ evaluates social biases in question answering [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.08193), BOLD studies open-ended generation across demographic prompts [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.11718), and RealToxicityPrompts examines toxic degeneration [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.11462). Regard-based analysis quantifies polarity disparities toward demographic groups in generated text [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.01326). Broader trust and holistic evaluation suites embed fairness alongside robustness, calibration, and privacy [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.09110), [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.11698). Recent surveys consolidate this rapidly growing literature and taxonomize evaluation metrics, datasets, and mitigation techniques for LLM fairness [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.00770), [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.01349). Almost all of these benchmarks, however, reduce fairness to differences in discrete accuracy or generation statistics, ignoring the distributional information carried by model confidence.

## Uncertainty Quantification and Calibration

The neural-network community has long recognized that a model's predicted probabilities carry information beyond top-1 accuracy. Modern deep networks are known to be miscalibrated, motivating post-hoc calibration techniques such as temperature scaling [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.04599). Bayesian approximations via dropout [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/1506.02142) and deep ensembles [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/1612.01474) provide scalable predictive-uncertainty estimates. For LLMs specifically, empirical studies have examined how uncertainty behaves in pre-trained language models across tasks [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.04714), whether models "know what they know" through self-evaluation and P(True) style probing [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.05221), and how semantic equivalence of generated outputs can be aggregated into semantic entropy [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.09664). Our UCerF metric builds on this line of work by combining group-conditional confidence with correctness to expose bias that is hidden from accuracy-only fairness measures — for example, high confidence in incorrect predictions concentrated in one demographic group.

## Modern Open-Source LLMs Under Evaluation

The models we benchmark span the current generation of open-weights LLMs, whose few-shot and instruction-following abilities have grown rapidly since GPT-3 [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165). Representative examples include LLaMA [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.13971) and Mistral-7B [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.06825), for which we show that conventional accuracy-based fairness diagnostics miss critical confidence-driven biases that UCerF surfaces.
