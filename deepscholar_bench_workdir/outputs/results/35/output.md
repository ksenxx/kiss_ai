# Related Work

**Diffusion and score-based generative models.** Denoising diffusion probabilistic models [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2006.11239) and their continuous-time score-based counterparts [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.13456), together with accelerated non-Markovian samplers such as DDIM [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.02502), have become the dominant paradigm for generative modeling of complex distributions. Their ability to model rich, multi-modal densities in high-dimensional spaces makes them a natural fit for protein structure, where the target distribution is intrinsically continuous and geometrically constrained.

**Generative models for protein structure.** Building on this foundation, a series of SE(3)-equivariant diffusion and flow-matching models have been developed for generating physically plausible protein backbones. FoldingDiff [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.15611) frames backbone generation as diffusion over torsion angles, EigenFold [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.02198) performs harmonic diffusion for structure prediction, FrameDiff [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.02277) introduces SE(3) diffusion over residue frames, Genie [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.12485) diffuses oriented residue clouds, and FoldFlow [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.02391) generalizes these ideas via stochastic flow matching on SE(3). While these methods primarily target de novo design and single-structure prediction, they establish the geometric backbones on which conformation samplers, including ours, are built.

**Protein conformation and ensemble generation.** Predicting a single crystallographic structure does not capture the dynamic ensembles that underlie protein function. Distributional Graphormer (DiG) [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05445) explicitly targets equilibrium distributions of molecular systems, Str2Str [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.03117) repurposes structure predictors as zero-shot conformation samplers, and AlphaFlow/ESMFlow [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.04845) re-cast AlphaFold and ESMFold as flow-matching generators to sample MD-like ensembles. ConfDiff [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.14088) further introduces force guidance into SE(3) diffusion for conformation generation, while MDGen [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.17808) generates entire molecular dynamics trajectories. These works form the closest benchmark set for our approach, and we compare against them on standard MD ensemble evaluations.

**Boltzmann generators and physics-aware learning.** A complementary line of work seeks to sample from the Boltzmann distribution directly. Boltzmann Generators [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/1812.01729) pioneered the use of normalizing flows trained with energy-based objectives; Timewarp [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.01170) learns time-coarsened MD dynamics, coarse-grained diffusion has been coupled with force fields [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.00600), and equivariant flow matching [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.15030) together with transferable Boltzmann generators [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.14426) push toward transferability across systems. High-accuracy machine-learned force fields such as NequIP [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.03164) and MACE [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.07697), reviewed extensively in [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.07067), provide the physical signals that make energy-based supervision practical at scale. Our method integrates such physical feedback into a pretrained diffusion model without requiring differentiation through intractable energy landscapes.

**Aligning generative models with feedback.** Our approach is inspired by preference and reward-based fine-tuning developed for language and image models. RLHF-style optimization [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02155) and its offline variant Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18290) reformulate alignment as a supervised classification problem over paired samples. These ideas have been adapted to diffusion models through reinforcement learning [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.13301), policy-gradient fine-tuning [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16381), differentiable reward backpropagation such as AlignProp [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03739) and DRaFT [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17400), learned reward models like ImageReward [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.05977), and preference-based Diffusion-DPO [29](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12908). Our Energy-based Alignment (EBA) draws on this preference-optimization perspective but replaces human preferences with physical energy differences, yielding a tractable objective that calibrates protein conformation samplers against physics-based feedback.
