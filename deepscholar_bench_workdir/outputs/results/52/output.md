# Related Work

## Dense Retrievers and Contrastive Fine-tuning

Dense retrieval reformulates first-stage retrieval as encoding queries and passages into low-dimensional vectors whose similarity is computed with a shallow operator such as inner product. Dense Passage Retrieval (DPR) [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.04906) established this paradigm by fine-tuning a BERT [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04805) dual-encoder with in-batch contrastive learning on question–passage pairs, using the [CLS] token as the sequence representation. Follow-up efforts have focused on improving the fine-tuning signal, particularly through harder negatives: ANCE [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00808) periodically refreshes an ANN index to mine globally hard negatives, and RocketQA [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.08191) combines cross-batch negatives with denoised hard negatives from a cross-encoder. ColBERT [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.12832) offers an alternative to single-vector encoding by preserving contextualized token embeddings and applying late interaction. Sentence-BERT [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/1908.10084) introduced siamese fine-tuning with mean-pooled sentence embeddings, and SimCSE [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08821) showed that simple contrastive objectives yield strong sentence encoders, motivating the mean-pooling variant that we study alongside DPR-style [CLS] encoders.

## Pre-training Objectives Tailored to Retrieval

A second line of work argues that generic masked language modeling is not ideal for retrieval and proposes retrieval-oriented pre-training. Condenser [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08253) restructures BERT so that the [CLS] token must aggregate information sufficient to reconstruct masked tokens, yielding a representation better suited to dot-product matching. Contriever [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.09118) uses unsupervised contrastive pre-training with mean-pooled representations, and cpt-text [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.10005) and E5 [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.03533) scale weakly supervised contrastive pre-training over large web pairs. Larger dual encoders such as GTR [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.07899) and instruction-tuned INSTRUCTOR [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.09741) demonstrate that scaling the backbone and diversifying supervision improve generalization on the BEIR benchmark [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663). These results emphasize that the pre-training recipe—not only the fine-tuning data—shapes downstream retrieval effectiveness, motivating our comparison across CLS/mean-pooled encoders and different backbones.

## Decoder-only Backbones for Dense Retrieval

More recently, decoder-only large language models have been adapted for dense retrieval. RepLLaMA [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.08319) fine-tunes LLaMA [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.13971) as a bi-encoder using the end-of-sequence hidden state, while LLaRA/Llama2Vec [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.15503) proposes unsupervised objectives that adapt causal LLMs into retrieval-friendly encoders. These models routinely outperform BERT-based DPR baselines but also reopen the question of whether their gains stem from pre-training scale or from fine-tuning, which is precisely the question we probe by contrasting encoder-only and decoder-only dense retrievers.

## The Role of Pre-training vs. Fine-tuning in Retrieval

Understanding where retrieval "knowledge" resides in these models has been the subject of active study. Petroni et al. [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.01066) showed that pre-trained language models already encode substantial factual knowledge that can be surfaced without fine-tuning, and a broader BERTology synthesis [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.12327) documents how pre-training shapes linguistic and world knowledge in encoders. Zero-shot studies of dense retrieval [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.12755) find that DPR-style models often fail to transfer to out-of-domain corpora, while few-shot approaches such as Promptagator [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11755) suggest that limited task-specific supervision suffices when a strong pre-trained backbone is available. Most directly relevant to our work, Reichman and Heck [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.11035) mechanistically analyze DPR and argue that fine-tuning primarily redistributes activations over knowledge already stored during pre-training, rather than instilling new retrieval-relevant knowledge. Their conclusion, however, is drawn from a BERT-based DPR trained on Natural Questions with [CLS] pooling. We extend this analysis to mean-pooled Contriever-style [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.09118) encoders, decoder-only LLaMA-based retrievers [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.08319), and MS MARCO [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.09268) supervision, situating our findings within the broader retrieval-augmented generation literature [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401) that depends on such dense retrievers for grounding.
