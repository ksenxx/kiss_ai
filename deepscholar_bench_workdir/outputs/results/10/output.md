# Related Work

## Next-token prediction and language model pre-training

Modern language models are almost universally trained with the next-token prediction (NTP) objective on top of the Transformer architecture [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762), and scaling this recipe with data and parameters has been the primary driver of downstream capability improvements [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165). Scaling-law studies [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.08361), [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.15556) have quantified the compute–parameter–data trade-offs of NTP pre-training, motivating a search for richer objectives that can improve sample efficiency without changing the underlying Transformer trunk. Our work follows this line by keeping NTP as the ultimate downstream objective, but altering the pre-training loss to enrich the signal that small language models (SLMs) receive during training.

## Multi-token prediction as a pre-training objective

The idea of predicting more than one token per position dates back at least to blockwise parallel decoding, which trained auxiliary heads to jointly predict a fixed block of future tokens for autoregressive Transformers [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.03115). Recently, Gloeckle et al. revisited this idea at scale and showed that adding $k$ independent prediction heads on top of a shared trunk (multi-token prediction, MTP) improves downstream performance and inference throughput, with benefits that grow with model size but that shrink or disappear for smaller models [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.19737). MTP has since been adopted as an auxiliary training objective by frontier systems such as DeepSeek-V3, which uses sequential MTP modules to densify the training signal and to enable speculative decoding at inference [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437). Complementary analyses have investigated whether MTP capabilities already exist implicitly in NTP-trained models and whether MTP heads can be grafted onto frozen backbones, finding that late layers of NTP-trained models are strongly specialized for one-step prediction and that joint training is required to make MTP work well [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.09419). These observations, together with the SLM difficulty reported in [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.19737), directly motivate our curriculum strategies for smoothly transitioning between NTP and MTP.

## Speculative decoding and multi-head drafters

A major application of MTP is to accelerate inference through speculative decoding, in which a fast drafter proposes several tokens that are then verified in parallel by a target model without changing the output distribution [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.17192). Medusa attaches multiple decoding heads to a base LLM to draft several future tokens simultaneously, which is architecturally very close to MTP heads [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.10774). Hydra improves on Medusa by making the draft heads sequentially dependent [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.05109), while EAGLE performs drafting in the feature space to better model uncertainty [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.15077). Other approaches remove the separate drafter entirely: Lookahead decoding parallelises n-gram generation from the target model itself [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02057), REST uses retrieval to provide draft continuations [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.08252), and Sequoia proposes hardware-aware tree drafting for robust speedups across model scales [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.12374). Our work shares Medusa-style multi-head drafting but focuses on the pre-training objective rather than a post-hoc drafter, and we evaluate self-speculative decoding using the same heads that were trained during pre-training.

## Self-speculative decoding

Self-speculative decoding removes the need for a separate draft model by having the target model draft its own continuations. Draft & Verify skips layers to produce a lightweight draft from the same weights and verifies it with the full network [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.08168). LayerSkip trains the model with layer dropout and early-exit losses so that shallow exits produce useful drafts that are then verified by later layers [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16710). Kangaroo introduces a double-early-exit mechanism to further reduce drafting overhead while preserving losslessness [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.18911). MTP heads similarly enable self-speculative decoding without a second model, and we show that a forward curriculum preserves this property in SLMs while a reverse curriculum does not.

## Curriculum learning for language models

Curriculum learning, the practice of ordering training data or objectives from easier to harder (or vice-versa), has a long history in deep learning and has been surveyed extensively [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.10382). Closest to our setting, sequence-length warm-up for GPT-style pre-training has been shown to stabilise optimisation and improve compute-efficiency, demonstrating that gradually increasing the difficulty of the pre-training signal can help even large-scale language models [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.06084). Our forward and reverse curricula extend this idea to the *objective* dimension by interpolating between NTP and MTP, and to the small-model regime where MTP has previously been reported to hurt performance [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.19737).
