# Related Work

## Neural Retrieval Models and Benchmarks

Neural retrieval has become the dominant paradigm for Web and passage search. Dense bi-encoder architectures such as DPR [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.04906) established that dual-encoder Transformers fine-tuned with in-batch contrastive objectives can outperform sparse baselines on open-domain QA. Follow-up work has pushed retrieval quality further with harder negatives and better training procedures, including ANCE, which mines negatives from an approximate nearest-neighbor index of the corpus [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00808), and RocketQA, which combines cross-batch negatives, denoised hard negatives, and data augmentation [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.08191). Sparse neural rankers such as SPLADE learn contextualized lexical expansions for first-stage retrieval [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.05720). Evaluation of these systems is standardized on MS~MARCO [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.09268) and, for out-of-domain generalization, on BEIR [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.08663). The size and richness of modern Web collections such as ClueWeb22 [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.15848), together with public retrieval baselines built on top of it [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.04357), motivate scalable, label-free training pipelines like the one we study.

## Synthetic Query Generation for Retriever Training

A long line of work exploits query generation as a source of weak supervision. Doc2Query trains a seq2seq model to predict queries a document could answer and expands the document with them [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/1904.08375), while domain-targeted synthetic question generation was shown to enable zero-shot neural passage retrieval [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.14503). More recently, large language models have been used to synthesize training queries directly. InPars few-shot-prompts a large LM to generate query–document pairs and trains rerankers on them [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.05144); InPars-v2 refines this pipeline with open-source LLMs and reaches state-of-the-art results on BEIR [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.01820); InPars-Light demonstrates that cost-effective unsupervised training with smaller open models is feasible [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.02998). Promptagator shows that just eight in-domain examples suffice to prompt an LM to generate task-specific queries for dense retrieval [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11755). GPL couples query generation with cross-encoder pseudo-labeling for unsupervised domain adaptation of dense retrievers [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.07577), UDAPDR distills LLM-prompted rerankers into efficient retrievers under distribution shift [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.00807), and AugTriever scales unsupervised dense retrieval through extractive and generative query augmentation [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.08841). Contriever also shows that contrastive pretraining alone yields strong unsupervised dense retrievers, providing a natural backbone for synthetic-data fine-tuning [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.09118).

## Quality Control via Rerankers and Zero-Shot LLM Scoring

Because synthetic queries vary greatly in quality, most of the pipelines above filter or reweight query–document pairs using an external re-ranker. Sequence-to-sequence rerankers such as monoT5 provide relevance scores by asking a T5 model whether a passage is relevant [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.06713), and are widely used as filters in InPars-style pipelines. Zero-shot LLM re-rankers, including UPR, which scores passages by the likelihood of regenerating the query [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.07496), listwise LLM re-rankers such as RankGPT [21](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.09542), and pairwise ranking prompting methods [22](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.17563), further extend the tools available for judging synthetic pairs. Query-side augmentation techniques such as HyDE [23](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.10496) and Query2Doc [24](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.07678) show that LLM-generated pseudo-documents can also carry useful ranking signal. In contrast to post-hoc filtering, our approach uses ranker judgments as a training signal so that the generator itself learns to produce retrieval-friendly queries.

## Preference Optimization for Language Models

Aligning generative models with downstream objectives has been extensively studied under RLHF, where InstructGPT combines supervised fine-tuning with PPO-based reward maximization [25](https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.02155) [26](https://arxiv.org/abs/1707.06347). Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [27](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18290) removes the explicit reward model and shows that preference data can be used to fine-tune LMs via a simple classification-style loss, making preference optimization practical outside of pure RLHF settings. Closest to our work, RetPO fine-tunes an LM to rewrite conversational queries using preference pairs derived from retriever feedback [28](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.11827). We build on this line of work by applying DPO to synthetic query generation for Web-scale retriever training, aligning the generator with re-ranker preferences over ClueWeb22 passages and evaluating downstream effectiveness on MS~MARCO.
