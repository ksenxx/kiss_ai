# Related Works

## Representation Similarity and Layer-wise Analysis

Understanding how internal representations evolve across the depth of neural networks has a long history that predates modern LLMs. Canonical correlation analysis was first adapted to deep learning as SVCCA [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.05806), providing a tool for tracking learning dynamics and inter-layer similarity in deep networks. Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) was subsequently introduced as a more reliable similarity index that is invariant to orthogonal transformations and isotropic scaling, and that can identify meaningful correspondences between representations across different initializations [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.00414). CKA and related metrics have since become standard for probing hidden-state dynamics inside Transformer-based [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) language models, and our work builds directly on this tradition by using CKA to identify data-oblivious change points across layers.

## Interpreting Transformer Layers

A complementary line of work seeks to interpret what individual Transformer layers compute. Voita et al. analyze the bottom-up evolution of token representations under different training objectives, showing that layers encode progressively higher-level linguistic abstractions [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/1909.01380). Geva et al. reveal that feed-forward blocks operate as key–value memories that store factual and lexical patterns [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.14913), while Dar et al. project attention and FFN parameters directly into the embedding space to explain layer function [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.02535). The Tuned Lens refines the "logit lens" idea and traces how next-token predictions are progressively refined layer by layer [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.08112). Meng et al. localize factual associations to mid-layer feed-forward modules via causal tracing, motivating the ROME model-editing method [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.05262). Recent work also documents surprising phenomena such as massive activations concentrated in a small number of layers and tokens [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.17762), and shows that intermediate layers—not the final ones—often yield the most useful representations for downstream tasks [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.09563). Representation engineering pushes this perspective further, treating high-level concepts as directions in the hidden-state space that can be read out and manipulated for transparency and control [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01405). In contrast to these largely data-dependent, post-hoc analyses, we identify change-point layers directly from a pre-fine-tuned model's spectral dynamics, independent of task data.

## Critical Layers and Efficient Fine-Tuning

A growing body of work argues that fine-tuning affects layers unevenly and that focusing updates on a subset of "critical" layers is both possible and desirable. LISA introduces layerwise importance sampling to make LLM fine-tuning memory-efficient by activating only a few layers per step [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.17919). Gradient-based analyses further reveal that different layers are updated with markedly different magnitudes when training for "fast" versus "slow" reasoning, indicating that specific layers carry the dominant learning signal [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.23743). Ren and Sutherland study the learning dynamics of LLM fine-tuning and characterize how updates propagate through the network, providing a theoretical grounding for layer-selective adaptation [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.10490). All of these methods, however, require access to the fine-tuning data or gradients to determine which layers matter. Our data-oblivious criterion, derived purely from representation dynamics of the pre-fine-tuned model, complements this line of work by predicting the same critical layers before any task-specific data is observed.

## Safety, Backdoors, and Layer-Level Defense

Layer-wise analysis has also become central to LLM safety and adversarial robustness. Qi et al. show that even benign fine-tuning of aligned models can strip safety behavior [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.03693), while gradient-based attacks such as GCG [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.15043) can universally jailbreak aligned LLMs; red-teaming studies further map the harms these attacks can elicit [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.07858). Recent defenses increasingly operate at the layer level: BEEAR removes backdoor triggers via embedding-space adversarial training [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.17092), and layer-specific editing has been proposed as a defense against jailbreaks by surgically modifying only a small set of vulnerable layers [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.18166). In the federated setting, Zhuang et al. show that poisoning a handful of "backdoor-critical" layers is sufficient to implant persistent triggers, underscoring how sensitive backdoor behavior is to a narrow subset of layers [20](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.04466). Our findings echo and extend this trend: freezing the data-oblivious critical layers identified via CKA and spectral analysis substantially reduces backdoor attack success rates during fine-tuning, providing a principled, pre-training-only way to select which layers to protect.
