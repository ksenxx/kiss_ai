# Related Work

## Industrial Image Anomaly Detection

Visual anomaly detection for industrial quality control has attracted extensive attention in recent years, in large part fueled by benchmark datasets such as MVTec-AD and the more recent VisA dataset introduced together with the SPot-the-Difference self-supervised pre-training scheme [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.14315). Comprehensive surveys of the field show that most academic pipelines are built around pre-trained backbones and unsupervised or one-class formulations, with relatively little attention paid to on-device deployment constraints [2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.11514).

## Embedding- and Memory-Based Approaches

A first family of methods models the distribution of features extracted from a pre-trained CNN and flags out-of-distribution activations as anomalies. PaDiM represents each spatial patch with a multivariate Gaussian over ImageNet features and localises defects via Mahalanobis distance [3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.08785). PatchCore extends this idea by building a coreset-subsampled memory bank of patch features, achieving state-of-the-art detection accuracy on MVTec-AD but at the cost of very large memory footprints and expensive nearest-neighbour searches [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.08265). Normalising-flow variants such as CFLOW-AD [5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2107.12571) and FastFlow [6](https://arxiv.org/abs/2111.07677) instead learn an explicit density over deep features conditioned on spatial position, providing tractable likelihoods and pixel-level anomaly maps in near real time.

## Reconstruction- and Distillation-Based Approaches

A second research line frames anomaly detection as a reconstruction or knowledge-transfer problem. The Uninformed Students paradigm trains an ensemble of students to regress teacher embeddings; large discrepancies at test time indicate anomalies [7](https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.02357). Reverse Distillation flips this by learning a decoder that reconstructs teacher features from a one-class embedding, so that reconstruction gaps become anomaly cues [8](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.10703). DRAEM learns a discriminative reconstruction–segmentation network on synthetically corrupted images [9](https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.07610), while DeSTSeg couples denoising student–teacher features with an explicit segmentation head to sharpen defect masks [10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.11317). More recent diffusion-based methods such as DiffusionAD use norm-guided one-step denoising to reconstruct anomaly-free counterparts of the query image [11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.08730). SimpleNet demonstrates that a lightweight feature adapter combined with a synthetic-anomaly discriminator can rival more elaborate pipelines [12](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.15140), and EfficientAD pushes runtime to millisecond-level latencies while preserving competitive AUROC on MVTec-AD, becoming a strong reference point for efficiency-oriented anomaly detectors like the one proposed in this paper [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14535).

## Foundation Segmentation Models and Their Efficient Variants

Our approach builds on the Segment Anything Model (SAM), a promptable segmentation foundation model trained on the SA-1B dataset that generalises to a wide range of downstream tasks [14](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.02643). Because the original ViT-H SAM is far too heavy for edge deployment, a number of lightweight variants have been proposed. FastSAM re-casts the task as instance segmentation with a YOLOv8-based backbone, achieving order-of-magnitude speed-ups [15](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.12156). MobileSAM instead decouples the image encoder from the mask decoder and distils SAM's ViT-H encoder into a Tiny-ViT, producing a model roughly 60× smaller while retaining most of SAM's zero-shot quality; this makes it particularly attractive for embedded platforms such as the NVIDIA Jetson series that we target [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.14289).

## SAM for Anomaly Detection

Several concurrent works have started to exploit SAM specifically for industrial inspection. STLM introduces a SAM-guided two-stream lightweight student network that distils SAM's rich representations into a compact model suitable for mobile devices [17](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.19145). SAM-LAD leverages SAM's zero-shot masks to reason about object-level logical anomalies without any training on defect images [18](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.00625). More recently, promptable anomaly segmentation methods such as SPT-SAM adapt SAM to the industrial domain via parameter-efficient tuning, addressing the domain gap between natural and factory imagery [19](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.17217). Compared with these works, our KairosAD focuses on the practical deployment scenario of Small and Medium Enterprises: it combines MobileSAM's efficient encoder [16](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.14289) with a supervised anomaly head that is dramatically smaller and faster than PatchCore [4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2106.08265) and EfficientAD [13](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.14535), while remaining competitive in AUROC on MVTec-AD and VisA [1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2207.14315) and running natively on Jetson-class embedded hardware.
